02 ‚Äî Anatomy & Function of the Eye
üî¨ Cornea
  • Clear tissue at the front of the eye
  • Refracts incoming light
  • Provides ~2/3 of the eye's optical power
  • Refractive power comes from difference between cornea and air
  • üèä When swimming, water eliminates cornea's refractive power
‚ö´ Pupil & Iris
  • Pupil = opening in the iris
  • Opening/closing controls amount of light entering the eye
  • Dilated pupil ‚Üí more light, dim environment vision
  • Constricted pupil ‚Üí
    • Reduces optical aberrations
    • Increases depth of field
  • Pupil appears black because all underlying structures are clear; retinal pigment epithelium behind is dark
üîç Lens
  • Focuses incoming light on the retina
  • Ciliary muscles change lens shape ‚Üí changes refractive power
  • Thin lens ‚Üí distant objects
  • Thick lens ‚Üí close objects (more refractive power)
  • Process = Accommodation
Accommodation = ability to tune focal plane
üß† Retina
  • Light-sensing neural tissue at the back of the eye
  • Parses incoming light into different visual channels (color, movement, edges, etc.)
  • Light passes through entire retina before hitting photoreceptors
üü§ Retinal Pigment Epithelium & Other Layers
  • RPE ‚Äî dark layer behind retina (makes pupil look black)
  • Choroid ‚Äî vascular layer beneath retina
  • Sclera ‚Äî outer white coat of the eye
  • Vitreous humor ‚Äî gel-filled posterior chamber
  • Aqueous humor ‚Äî fluid in anterior chamber
  • RPE phagocytoses shed photoreceptor outer segments
üéØ Fovea & Optic Nerve
  • Fovea = highest resolution vision region
  • Macula = central retina responsible for high-res vision; contains the fovea
  • Optic disk = nerve fibers leaving the eye ‚Üí blind spot
  • Optic nerve = bundle of retinal ganglion cell axons
  • Carries retinal signals to rest of brain
03 ‚Äî Optics & Accommodation
üì∑ Pupil Size & Depth of Field
Large Aperture / Dilated Pupil
  • More light enters
  • Narrow depth of field
  • Foreground/background blurry
  • Used in dim environments
Small Aperture / Constricted Pupil
  • Less light enters
  • Good depth of field
  • Sharp focus extends widely
  • Fewer optical aberrations
üìÖ Accommodation & Aging
  • As we age, lens loses elasticity
  • Far objects ‚Üí relax eye muscles ‚Üí thin lens
  • Near objects ‚Üí contract muscles ‚Üí thick lens
  • With age, near objects can't be focused well
  • Presbyopia = age-related loss of near focus
  • May require reading glasses to compensate
Accommodation amplitude declines steadily from age 15‚Äì70+
04 ‚Äî Vision Disorders
‚úÖ Emmetropia (Normal)
  • Image focused directly on retina
  • Lens accommodation works correctly
  • Clear vision at all distances
üëì Myopia (Nearsightedness)
  • Images focused in front of the retina
  • Far away objects appear blurry
  • Near objects seen clearly
  • Affects ~50% of people
  • Corrected with concave lenses
Most common refractive error globally
üî≠ Hyperopia (Farsightedness)
  • Images focused (virtually) behind the retina
  • Close objects appear blurry
  • Distant objects seen more clearly
  • Corrected with convex lenses
üåÄ Astigmatism
  • Arises from spherical aberrations of the eye
  • Different focal points for different parts of visual field
  • Vision blurry at multiple distances/orientations
  • Usually corrected with glasses or contact lenses

‚≠ï The Blind Spot
  • Optic disk has no photoreceptors ‚Üí blind spot (scotoma)
  • Visual system "fills in" the scotoma automatically
  • Mechanism not completely understood
  • You don't normally perceive the blind spot
üéØ Fovea Structure & Visual Acuity
  • Exclusively contains densely packed cone photoreceptors
  • ~200-fold increase in cone density vs. periphery
  • High acuity achieved by:
    • Foveal cones are skinnier and more densely packed
    • Other retinal cells pushed to the side
    • Fovea is avascular (no blood vessels obstructing)
  • Outside fovea: rods vastly outnumber cones

06 ‚Äî Photoreceptors: Rods vs. Cones
üåô Rods
  • Function under dark (scotopic) conditions
  • Do not confer colour vision
  • Express a single opsin (rhodopsin)
  • More sensitive to light than cones
  • Relatively spread out ‚Üí low resolution vision
  • Monochromatic only
  • Located mainly in peripheral retina
Dominant at starlight/moonlight levels
‚òÄÔ∏è Cones
  • Function under bright (photopic) conditions
  • Confer colour vision (multiple opsins)
  • Provide high-resolution central vision
  • Make up the fovea
  • Less sensitive to light than rods
Dominant from indoor lighting to sunlight levels
‚ö° Photoreceptor Signaling
  • Signal via graded membrane potentials (not action potentials)
  • Graded potentials signal intensity via amplitude and length
  • Action potentials not needed (distances in retina are short)
  • Graded potentials enable analog signaling
  • In the dark: relatively depolarized, release glutamate
  • In the light: hyperpolarize, release less glutamate

  • At 50% bleach level ‚Äî most photopigment bleached by bright light

KEY MOLECULES
  • Retinal ‚Äî vitamin A derivative; the light-absorbing chromophore
  • Opsin ‚Äî protein component; determines wavelength specificity
  • Photopigment = retinal + opsin
  • Rhodopsin ‚Äî the opsin in rods (peak ~498nm)
  • Transducin ‚Äî G-protein activated by rhodopsin
  • PDE (Phosphodiesterase) ‚Äî hydrolyzes cGMP
AMPLIFICATION FACTS
  • 1 rhodopsin ‚Üí ~800 transducin molecules
  • 1 transducin ‚Üí 1 PDE
  • 1 PDE ‚Üí ~6 cGMP hydrolyzed
  • 1 photon ‚Üí ~200 channels closed
  • ~2% of dark-open channels closed per photon
  • Result: 1 mV hyperpolarization
üõë Stopping the Cascade

  • Arrestin prevents rhodopsin from activating more transducin

üîÅ The Retinoid Cycle (Chromophore Recycling)
  • All-trans retinal dissociates from opsin ‚Üí diffuses into outer segment cytosol

08 ‚Äî Color Vision & Color Blindness
üåà Three Cone Types & Spectral Sensitivity
400nm (Violet) 500nm (Cyan) 600nm (Yellow) 700nm (Red)
420nm S Cone Blue light ~10% of cones Uncommon in foveal center
498nm Rod Rhodopsin Single opsin Scotopic
534nm M Cone Green light Medium wavelength
564nm L Cone Red light Long wavelength
  • M and L cone ratio varies person-to-person (~1:1 to 4:1) with no significant impact on colour vision
  • Specific wavelength-sensitive photoreceptors alone do not produce color vision
  • Color vision arises from comparing & contrasting responses across cone types
üé® Colour Contrast & Context
  • Context and colour contrast significantly affect colour perception
  • Same physical wavelength can appear different colours depending on surrounding context
  • Classic demonstration: Rubik's cube illusion ‚Äî same tile appears brown vs. orange depending on lighting/shadow context
The brain uses surrounding information to infer object colour
üî¥üü¢ Red-Green Color Blindness
  • Genes for red and green opsins are on the X chromosome
  • ~8% of XY (male) population is color blind
  • Protanopia ‚Äî missing L (red) cone
  • Deuteranopia ‚Äî missing M (green) cone
  • Both types have difficulty distinguishing red from green
  • Diagnosed using Ishihara plates (dot patterns with hidden numbers)
Much rarer in XX (female) population ‚Äî need defect on both X chromosomes
‚¨õ Achromatopsia (Complete Color Blindness)
  • Complete colorblindness from total lack of functioning cones
  • Can also arise from brain damage to color processing area
  • Prevalence: ~1 in 30,000 people
  • Island of Pingelap (South Pacific):
    • ~10% of population are achromatopes
    • 1775: typhoon wiped out most inhabitants
    • Population rebuilt largely by king who was himself an achromatope
    • ‚Üí Genetic bottleneck effect
  • Oliver Sacks wrote "The Island of the Colorblind" about this
üëÅÔ∏è Trichromacy vs. Dichromacy
  • Trichromat (normal) ‚Äî 3 functional cone types (S, M, L)
  • Protanope ‚Äî missing L cone; S + M only
  • Deuteranope ‚Äî missing M cone; S + L only
  • Color vision requires comparison between at least 2 cone types
  • Single cone type alone ‚Üí cannot distinguish wavelength from intensity
