01 ‚Äî Building Blocks of the Nervous System
üß†Neuron
  • Cell body (soma) ‚Äî contains nucleus, metabolic hub
  • Dendrites ‚Äî receive messages from other cells
  • Axon ‚Äî passes messages away from soma to other neurons, muscles, or glands
  • Myelin sheath ‚Äî covers axon of some neurons; speeds neural impulses
  • Action potential ‚Äî electrical signal traveling down the axon
  • Terminal buttons ‚Äî form junctions (synapses) with other cells
üîóNeural Circuit
  • Diverse subsets of neurons form ensembles called neural circuits
  • Circuits are the primary components of neural systems
  • Each circuit processes a specific type of information
‚öôÔ∏èNeural System
  • Collections of circuits dedicated to a unified function
  • Components distributed throughout body and brain
  • Includes both peripheral specializations and central structures
02 ‚Äî Neural Systems: Purposes & Principles
üëÅÔ∏èSensory Systems
  • Acquire & process information from internal & external environments
  • Include peripheral specializations: eye, ear, skin, nose
  • Use topographic maps (vision, touch) or computational maps (smell, taste)
ü¶æMotor Systems
  • Respond to information (esp. sensory) by generating movements
  • Include peripheral motor nerves and target muscles
üîÄAssociation Systems
  • Lie between input (sensory) and output (motor) systems
  • Include higher-order systems: language, emotion
  • Less well understood; may not follow neat sensory/motor organization
üó∫Ô∏èTopographic Maps
  • Systems distinguishing neighboring points (vision in visual field; touch on body surface) represent info topographically
  • Reflect a point-to-point correspondence between sensory periphery and CNS neurons
  • Example: somatosensory homunculus ‚Äî face, lips, tongue, jaw, hand, fingers, thumb occupy more cortex than trunk/limbs
üí°Core Principles of Neural Systems
  • Unity of function ‚Äî system = all neurons & connections dedicated to one function
  • Distributed components ‚Äî spread across body and brain
  • Orderly representation ‚Äî specific info represented at various levels
  • Parallel processing ‚Äî sub-modalities processed separately but simultaneously (e.g., frequency & volume of sound; color & motion of visual stimulus)
  • Computational maps ‚Äî smell & taste compare/integrate multiple stimulus attributes

04 ‚Äî Neuroanatomical Terminology
üî¨Collections of Neurons
  • Gray matter ‚Äî cell bodies in the brain; appear grey in freshly dissected brain
  • Cortex ‚Äî thin sheet of neurons, usually at brain's surface
  • Nucleus ‚Äî clearly distinguishable mass of neurons, usually deep in brain (Latin: "nut")
  • Substantia ‚Äî related neurons, less distinct boundaries than a nucleus
  • Locus (pl: loci) ‚Äî small, well-defined group of cells
  • Ganglion (pl: ganglia) ‚Äî collection of neurons in the PNS (Greek: "knot"); only the basal ganglia in CNS
üì°Collections of Axons
  • Nerve ‚Äî bundle of axons in PNS; only CNS nerve = optic nerve
  • White matter ‚Äî generic term for axon collections; appear white from myelination
  • Tract ‚Äî CNS axons with common origin AND destination
  • Bundle ‚Äî axons running together but NOT necessarily common origin/destination
  • Capsule ‚Äî axon collection connecting cerebrum with brainstem
  • Commissure ‚Äî axon collection connecting one side of brain to the other
  • Lemniscus ‚Äî tract that meanders through the brain like a ribbon
05 ‚Äî Methods to Study Neural Systems
üß¨Genetic Analysis
  • Genetic variation shapes structure & function of the nervous system
  • Human studies: genetic analysis in families with inherited diseases
  • Look for variation between affected & unaffected individuals
GWAS ‚Äî Genome Wide Association Studies
  • Large-scale population studies
  • Assesses statistical correlation between genetic variation & frequency of clinically diagnosed conditions
  • Identifies 'risk loci' for a particular condition
  • Example: Alzheimer's GWAS (Lambert et al. 2013)
Gene Expression
  • ~6,000 genes expressed exclusively in nervous system
  • ~8,000 genes expressed ubiquitously in all cell/tissue types
  • ~6,000 genes not expressed in nervous system
Animal Models
  • Candidate genes found in humans ‚Üí studied in Drosophila, zebrafish, C. elegans, mouse
  • Candidate genes ‚Üí cell models or animal models to understand biological function
üõ†Ô∏èGenetic Engineering
  • Introduce a new gene, or modify/disable existing gene in mouse embryonic stem cells in vitro
  • Gene ‚Üí incorporated into inner cell mass ‚Üí surgical transfer of embryos into foster mother
  • ‚Üí Mosaic (chimera) √ó wild type
  • If some manipulated cells produce egg/sperm ‚Üí Heterozygous (F‚ÇÅ) carrying one copy of manipulated gene
  • Mating two F‚ÇÅ ‚Üí Homozygous carrier (F‚ÇÇ)
  • Uses: drug/genetic screens, transplantation studies (in vivo), cellular studies (in vitro)
  • Also: iPS cells from patient cells ‚Üí neural, cardiac, beta cells, hepatocytes
üóÇÔ∏èStructural Analysis
Lesion Studies
  • Inferences of functional location by correlating post-mortem brain damage with functional deficits observed in life
  • Classic example: Henry Molaison (HM) ‚Äî bilateral hippocampectomy ‚Üí lost ability to form new lasting autobiographical memory
  • Limitations: biased recall of functional changes; uncontrolled damage
  • Animal studies: experimentally induced lesions ‚Äî still limited
Tract Tracing
  • Permits detailed assessment of connections between brain regions
  • Retrograde tracer ‚Äî injected at destination; travels backward to show which cells send connections TO the injection site
  • Anterograde tracer ‚Äî injected at origin; travels forward to show which regions RECEIVE connections from injection site
‚ö°Functional Analysis ‚Äî Invasive
  • Electrophysiological recording ‚Äî historically the most widely used method
  • Implant electrodes to record from single neurons or populations
  • Example: somatic sensory cortex ‚Äî touch in center of receptive field increases cell firing; touch in surround decreases cell firing; touch outside receptive field has no effect
üì°Functional Analysis ‚Äî Non-Invasive
  • EEG (Electroencephalography) ‚Äî scalp electrodes; measures electrical activity of populations of neurons
  • TMS (Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation) ‚Äî magnetic pulses temporarily disrupt or stimulate cortical areas
  • CT (Computed Tomography) ‚Äî structural X-ray imaging of brain
  • fMRI (Functional MRI) ‚Äî measures blood-oxygenation-level dependent (BOLD) signal as proxy for neural activity
06 ‚Äî Anatomical References & Planes
üê≠Directions ‚Äî Quadruped
  • In animals walking on all fours, body and brain directions are the same
  • Anterior / Rostral ‚Äî toward head/front
  • Posterior / Caudal ‚Äî toward tail/back
  • Dorsal ‚Äî toward back (spine)
  • Ventral ‚Äî toward belly
üßçDirections ‚Äî Human
  • Humans walk upright ‚Üí head at angle to body
  • Anterior, Posterior, Superior, Inferior ‚Äî refer to long axis of body (same for forebrain AND brainstem)
  • Forebrain: Dorsal = top of head; Rostral = toward face
  • Brainstem & Spinal cord: Dorsal = to the back; Rostral = toward top of head
  • Dorsal/ventral/rostral/caudal refer to long axis of CNS
‚úÇÔ∏èPlanes & Symmetry
  • Sagittal ‚Äî left/right division (vertical, front-to-back)
  • Horizontal / Axial ‚Äî top/bottom division
  • Coronal / Frontal ‚Äî front/back division
  • Cutting along midline reveals bilateral symmetry of the brain
Ipsilateral vs Contralateral
  • Contralateral ‚Äî on opposite sides of midline
  • Ipsilateral ‚Äî on same side of midline
  • Ex: Left visual cortex receives ipsilateral projections from left eye AND contralateral projections from right eye (via optic chiasm)
07 ‚Äî CNS Regions: Spinal Cord, Brainstem & Cerebellum
ü¶¥Spinal Cord ‚Äî External Anatomy
  • Peripheral nerves innervating the body arise from spinal nerves
  • Dorsal roots ‚Äî carry afferent (sensory) axons INTO cord
  • Ventral roots ‚Äî carry efferent (motor) axons OUT of cord
  • Once dorsal & ventral roots join, sensory & motor axons travel together in spinal nerves
  • Segments: Cervical (C1‚ÄìC8), Thoracic (T1‚ÄìT12), Lumbar (L1‚ÄìL5), Sacral (S1‚ÄìS5), Coccygeal
  • Cervical enlargement ‚Äî upper limb control
  • Lumbosacral enlargement ‚Äî lower limb control
  • Cauda equina ‚Äî nerve roots below spinal cord end
  • Also: dorsal root ganglion, layers of meninges, sympathetic chain
‚¨õSpinal Cord ‚Äî Internal Anatomy
  • Interior = gray matter (butterfly shape), surrounded by white matter
Gray Matter Horns
  • Dorsal horns ‚Äî receive sensory info entering via dorsal roots
  • Ventral horns ‚Äî contain cell bodies of motor neurons ‚Üí axons ‚Üí ventral roots ‚Üí skeletal muscles
  • Interneurons bridge sensory and motor circuits
White Matter Columns
  • Dorsal columns ‚Äî ascending sensory info from somatic mechanoreceptors ‚Üí medulla
  • Lateral columns ‚Äî axons from cerebral cortex to interneurons/motor neurons in ventral horns (lateral corticospinal tract)
  • Ventral columns ‚Äî ascending pain & temperature info; descending motor info from brainstem & motor cortex
üåâBrainstem & Cranial Nerves
  • = Midbrain + Pons + Medulla
  • Located between diencephalon and spinal cord
3 Fundamental Functions
  • ‚ë† Target & source for cranial nerves (sensory & motor of head & neck)
  • ‚ë° "Throughway": ascending sensory tracts from spinal cord; sensory tracts for head & neck; descending motor tracts from forebrain; local pathways linking eye movement centers
  • ‚ë¢ Regulates levels of consciousness through extensive forebrain projections
Organization
  • Brainstem structures are tightly packed
  • Cranial nerve nuclei = target of cranial sensory nerves AND source of cranial motor nerves
  • Sensory nuclei found laterally
  • Motor nuclei found more medially
Cerebellum
  • "Tiny brain" (Latin)
  • Contains as many neurons as the cerebrum
  • Primary role: movement control
08 ‚Äî Forebrain: Cortical & Surface Anatomy
üåêCerebral Hemispheres ‚Äî Overview
  • Generally receives & sends information to the contralateral side of the body
  • Surface = folds: Gyri (ridges) and Sulci (grooves/fissures)
  • Bilateral symmetry ‚Äî left and right hemispheres roughly mirror each other
  • Corpus callosum bridges the two hemispheres ‚Äî carries axons from cortical neurons to contact target neurons in the opposite cortical region
üó∫Ô∏èCortical Lobes
  • Frontal lobe ‚Äî motor, executive function, planning
  • Parietal lobe ‚Äî somatosensory, spatial processing
  • Temporal lobe ‚Äî auditory, language, memory (hippocampus)
  • Occipital lobe ‚Äî primary visual cortex
  • Insula ‚Äî hidden beneath frontal & temporal lobes
Key Sulci as Boundaries
  • Lateral (Sylvian) fissure ‚Äî separates temporal from frontal & parietal lobes
  • Central sulcus ‚Äî separates frontal and parietal lobes
  • Parieto-occipital sulcus ‚Äî separates parietal & occipital lobes
üìçKey Gyri
  • Precentral gyrus ‚Äî motor cortex (in front of central sulcus)
  • Postcentral gyrus ‚Äî somatosensory cortex (behind central sulcus)
  • Cingulate gyrus ‚Äî part of limbic system
  • Superior / Middle / Inferior frontal gyri
  • Superior / Middle / Inferior temporal gyri
  • Intraparietal sulcus
  • Paracentral lobule
  • Precuneus gyrus
  • Cuneus gyrus & Lingual gyrus (occipital)
  • Calcarine sulcus ‚Äî locates primary visual cortex
  • Preoccipital notch
üß≠Midsagittal Surface
  • Calcarine sulcus ‚Üí primary visual cortex
  • Cingulate gyrus & sulcus ‚Üí limbic system
  • Corpus callosum ‚Üí inter-hemispheric commissure
  • Anterior commissure ‚Üí connects two hemispheres
  • Parieto-occipital sulcus
  • Visible: Diencephalon, Brainstem structures (midbrain, pons, medulla), Cerebellum, Spinal cord
üî≠Dorsal & Ventral Views
  • Dorsal view ‚Äî frontal, parietal, occipital lobes visible; bilateral symmetry evident
  • Ventral view ‚Äî temporal lobe, optic chiasma, olfactory bulb visible
  • Dorsal view with cortex removed ‚Äî corpus callosum and cerebellum visible
  • Optic chiasma ‚Äî where optic nerve fibers partially cross; located on ventral surface
  • Olfactory bulb ‚Äî anterior, ventral surface
09 ‚Äî Diencephalon, Thalamus & Internal Forebrain
üéØDiencephalon
  • Part of the forebrain
  • Components: Thalamus and Hypothalamus
Thalamus
  • Relay of sensory & motor signals to relevant primary cortical areas
  • Distributer of high-order signals from one cortical area to another
  • Sensory pathways from eye, ear, skin all relay in thalamus before terminating in cortex
  • ~50 nuclear subdivisions each with distinct inputs & outputs
  • Receives input from throughout brain & spinal cord
  • Sends info to different cortical areas via internal capsule
  • Key nuclei: Anterior nuclear group, Thalamic reticular nucleus, Ventral anterior, Lateral dorsal, Ventral lateral, Lateral posterior, Ventral posterior lateral, Ventral posterior medial, Pulvinar, Medial geniculate complex, Lateral geniculate nucleus, Mediodorsal nucleus, Intralaminar nuclei, Internal medullary lamina, Midline thalamic nuclei
Hypothalamus
  • Homeostatic and reproductive functions
  • Visible on midsagittal section
  • Associated structures: mammillary body, optic chiasm
üè∫Internal Forebrain Anatomy
  • Amygdala ‚Äî located in front of hippocampus; temporal lobe; emotional processing
  • Hippocampus ‚Äî temporal horn of lateral ventricle; memory
Basal Ganglia
  • Caudate nucleus
  • Putamen
  • Globus pallidus
  • Role in movement control, reward, learning
White Matter Tracts
  • Internal capsule ‚Äî major pathway linking cerebral cortex to brainstem & spinal cord
  • Anterior commissure ‚Äî axon tract connecting two hemispheres
  • Fornix ‚Äî fiber tract connecting hippocampus to hypothalamus
  • Corpus callosum
Ventricles
  • Lateral ventricles (temporal horn visible)
  • Third ventricle
  • Tail of caudate nucleus
üîóCerebrum ‚Äî Key Facts
  • Generally receives & sends information to contralateral side of body
  • Surface: gray matter (cortex); depth: white matter
  • Connected to brainstem via internal capsule
Other Midsagittal Structures
  • Pineal gland
  • Posterior commissure
  • Superior colliculus (midbrain; visual)
  • Inferior colliculus (midbrain; auditory)
  • Cerebral aqueduct ‚Äî connects 3rd & 4th ventricles

10 ‚Äî Vascular System, Blood-Brain Barrier & Protection
ü©∏Blood Supply
  • Damage to major blood vessels by trauma or stroke ‚Üí combinations of functional deficits
  • Deficits caused by: local cell death AND disruption of axons passing through damaged area
  • Neurons are highly sensitive to oxygen (& glucose) deprivation ‚Üí high metabolic rate
  • Ischemia ‚Äî even brief loss of blood supply can cause cellular changes ending in cell death
  • Prolonged loss ‚Üí cell death & degeneration
  • Stroke ‚Äî death or dysfunction of brain tissue following compromised blood supply
üõ°Ô∏èBlood-Brain Barrier (BBB)
  • Protects the brain from toxins & fluctuations in ionic milieu
  • Interface between capillary walls and surrounding tissue ‚Äî present throughout body
  • In brain: tight junctions form between capillary endothelial cells ‚Äî NOT seen elsewhere in body
  • Astrocyte foot processes envelop capillaries and reinforce BBB
Crossing the BBB
  • Molecules must move through endothelial membranes (not between)
  • Must be lipid soluble
  • OR actively transported (e.g., glucose)
üßÖThe Meninges
  • Greek: "covering"
  • Membranes protecting brain & spinal cord from direct contact with skull or bone
3 Layers (outer ‚Üí inner)
  • Dura mater ‚Äî outermost; Latin "hard mother"
  • Arachnoid membrane ‚Äî middle; web-like; Greek "spider"; blood vessels pass between dura & arachnoid ‚Üí ruptures cause subdural hematomas; fluid build-up = dangerous (pressure on CNS)
  • Pia mater ‚Äî innermost; adheres closely to brain; includes many blood vessels; Latin "gentle mother"
Subarachnoid Space
  • Between pia and arachnoid
  • Contains cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)
11 ‚Äî Ventricular System, CSF & Glymphatic System
üíßVentricular System & CSF
  • Ventricles ‚Äî canals through the brain filled with CSF; provide useful anatomical landmarks
Ventricle Anatomy
  • Lateral ventricles (left & right) ‚Äî in cerebral hemispheres
  • Foramen of Monro ‚Äî connects lateral ventricles to 3rd ventricle
  • Third ventricle ‚Äî in diencephalon
  • Cerebral aqueduct ‚Äî connects 3rd to 4th ventricle
  • Fourth ventricle ‚Äî in brainstem/cerebellum
  • Foramen of Magendie ‚Äî exit of 4th ventricle to subarachnoid space
CSF Production & Flow
  • CSF produced by choroid plexus ‚Äî special tissue lining ventricles
  • Produces 500 ml CSF/day
  • Normal ventricular volume: 150 ml
  • CSF turns over multiple times daily
  • CSF flows through ventricles ‚Üí exits CNS via small openings along dorsal midline of forebrain ‚Üí subarachnoid space
  • Absorbed by arachnoid villi / subarachnoid villi into blood
  • Also visible: superior sagittal sinus, dura mater
üîÑGlymphatic System
  • The brain's waste clearance system
  • CSF passes from arterial perivascular space through substance of the brain (via astrocyte channels)
  • CSF rinses metabolic waste and discarded proteins (e.g., amyloid-beta)
  • Waste-carrying CSF passes out of brain via perivenous space surrounding veins
  • CSF flow increases during sleep when extracellular spaces expand
  • Structures involved: astrocyte end feet, convective flow channels, perivenous space, periarterial space
Significance
  • Links sleep quality to brain health ‚Äî poor sleep impairs waste clearance
  • Potentially important in neurodegenerative diseases (Alzheimer's)
