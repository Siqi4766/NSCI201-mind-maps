Sensation — General Introduction

What Is Sensation?

Sensation is the ability to transduce, encode, and ultimately perceive information generated by stimuli arising from both external and internal environments.

Five Basic Senses
  • Somatic — pressure, temperature, vibration, pain
  • Vision — light waves
  • Audition — sound waves
  • Taste — chemical
  • Smell — chemical senses
The Sixth Sense

Proprioception

The sense of where your body is in space — relative position of limbs and body parts.

Why "sixth"? Not part of classical five — arises from internal mechanoreceptors in muscles, tendons & joints.
Universal Rules of Sensation
  • Specialized receptors convert energy into afferent sensory signals
  • Signals convey: Modality (touch vs pain; sharp vs dull)
  • Signals convey: Location (where it is)
  • Signals convey: Intensity
  • Signals convey: Time Course
  • Understanding deficits = key to diagnosing neurological problems

Pseudounipolar Sensory Neurons

Bipolar Neuron (comparator)
  • Axon — carries signal away from cell body
  • Dendrites — receive incoming signals
  • Two distinct processes
Pseudounipolar Neuron
  • One axon with two branches — no true dendrites
  • Central branch → cell body → spinal cord (carries APs toward CNS)
  • Peripheral branch → cell body → periphery (skin / joint / muscle)
  • Found in Dorsal Root Ganglia and sensory ganglia of cranial nerves
  • Cell body is in DRG — axon exits DRG and splits
  • Central branch → dorsal horn of spinal cord
  • Peripheral branch → travels through spinal nerve to skin, joint, muscle
  • Electrical activity does not need to pass through the cell body membrane
  • Travels along the continuous peripheral + central axon
  • Also found in sensory ganglia of cranial nerves

Sensory Transduction — Converting Stimuli to Signals

Transduction Mechanism (Pacinian Corpuscle Example)
  • Stimulus → changes permeability of cation channels in afferent nerve endings
  • Channels open → Na⁺ flows in → depolarizing current
  • This generates a Receptor Potential (depolarizing current)
  • Same basic mechanism mediates transduction in all somatic sensory afferents
  • If threshold is reached → Action Potential (AP) generated in afferent fiber
  • Rate of AP firing is proportional to magnitude of depolarization
  • APs travel along peripheral axon past cell body in DRG → along central axon → synaptic terminals in spinal cord
Stimulus Strength → AP Rate Weak stimulus = subthreshold receptor potential (no AP). Moderate = threshold (minimal APs). Strong = large receptor potential = rapid AP firing.

Piezo Mechanotransduction Channels

Piezo1 & Piezo2
  • Evolutionarily conserved protein family
  • Encode mammalian mechanosensitive cation channels
  • Critical roles in: touch, pain, proprioception, vascular development, blood pressure regulation
  • Over 2500 amino acids with numerous predicted transmembrane segments
  • No sequence homology with any known class of ion channels
  • Convert mechanical force into electrochemical signals
Which Receptors Express Piezo?
  • Merkel cells AND their sensory afferents — both express Piezo2
  • Meissner corpuscles — express Piezo2
  • Key papers: Coste et al. (2010) Science; Wang & Xiao (2018) J Physiol
Piezo1 — structural features and molecular bases underlying ion permeation and mechanotransduction (Wang & Xiao, 2018 J Physiol 596:969–978)

Key Principle: Larger axon diameter → faster conduction of action potential
① Axon Diameter
  • Determines conduction speed
  • Larger diameter = faster AP
  • Proprioception: largest (Ia)
  • Pain C fibers: smallest (unmyelinated)
② Receptive Field
  • Area of skin where stimulation causes significant change in AP rate
  • Size varies by body region
  • Determined by: branching of afferents in skin + density of innervation
  • Smaller arborization → smaller RF
  • More afferents → smaller RF
  • Smaller RF = higher spatial acuity
③ Temporal Dynamics
  • Rapidly Adapting (RA): fire at stimulus onset; quiescent if maintained; may fire at offset
  • Slowly Adapting (SA): continue to fire with sustained stimulation
  • RA → important for changes in stimulation (movement)
  • SA → conveys spatial attributes (size, shape)
  • Determined by properties of mechanoreceptors
④ Quality of Stimuli
  • Different classes respond only to restricted set of stimuli (stretch, temp, vibration)
  • Determined by: channel properties + filter properties of mechanoreceptors
  • Different afferents = parallel pathways
  • Remain segregated even if they travel together initially
Two-Point Discrimination
  • Measures minimum distance between two stimuli perceived as distinct
  • Determined by receptive field size
  • Fingertips: ~2 mm (very high acuity)
  • Forearm: ~40 mm (low acuity)
  • Somatic acuity much higher in fingers, toes, face than arms, legs, torso
  • Species specific!
Body Map of Two-Point Threshold
  • Fingers 1–4: ~2–3 mm (smallest)
  • Palm / Lower arm: ~15–25 mm
  • Shoulder / Forehead / Cheek / Nose: ~15–25 mm
  • Upper lip: ~10 mm
  • Breast / Back / Belly / Thigh: ~30–40 mm
  • Calf / Sole: ~35–40 mm
  • Toe: ~10 mm
  • Left side ≈ right side (some asymmetry)

Touch — Cutaneous Sensation

Glabrous Skin
  • Best studied region for cutaneous sensation
  • Palm and fingertips
  • Specialized for high-definition neural image of manipulated objects
  • Contains 4 types of mechanoreceptors
Haptics
  • Active touching — interpretation of complex spatiotemporal stimulus patterns
  • Activates many classes of mechanoreceptors simultaneously
Stereognosis
  • Capacity to identify an object by manipulating it with the hand
  • Requires integration of all mechanoreceptor classes
  • Deficits indicate parietal lobe damage
Sensory Homunculus

A physical representation of body proportions as mapped in the somatosensory cortex — face and hands are grossly enlarged relative to their actual body proportion, reflecting dense innervation and large cortical representation.

Proprioception — Mechanoreceptors & Function

What Is Proprioception?
  • Information about mechanical forces arising within the body
  • Proprioceptors = low-threshold mechanoreceptors
  • Provide continuous, detailed info about position of limbs & body parts in space
  • Essential for accurate complex movements
  • Head position: integrated with vestibular system
Three Proprioceptor Types
Muscle Spindles Golgi Tendon Organs Joint Receptors
Vision & Proprioception Integration
  • Artificial vibration of spindles → sensory illusions of altered limb position in stationary limbs
  • Illusion only if visual input is prevented
  • Normally: proprioception achieved by integration of somatic & visual cues
  • Joint angle perception = integration of afferent signals from muscle spindles + efferent motor commands
Muscle Spindles
  • Found in striated (skeletal) muscle
  • 4–8 specialized intrafusal muscle fibers surrounded by connective tissue capsule
  • Distributed in parallel with extrafusal fibers
  • Sensory afferents coil around the central part of the intrafusal spindle
  • Muscle stretch → tension in intrafusal fibers → activates mechanically gated ion channels → APs

PRIMARY ENDINGS

  • Group Ia afferents (largest myelinated sensory axons)
  • Rapidly adapting responses to changes in muscle length
  • Transmit information about limb dynamics

SECONDARY ENDINGS

  • Group II afferents
  • Sustained responses to constant muscle lengths
  • Information about static limb position

EFFERENT CONTROL

  • Also innervated by efferent γ (gamma) motor neurons in ventral horn of spinal cord
  • γ neurons change intrafusal fiber tension → increase sensitivity of afferents to changes in muscle length
Spindle Density: Muscles generating coarse movement have fewer spindles than fine-movement muscles. More precise movement = more refined sensory input (eyes, hand, neck).
Golgi Tendon Organs
  • Low-threshold mechanoreceptors in tendons
  • Sense changes in muscle tension
  • Distributed along collagen fibers forming tendons
  • Arranged in series with extrafusal muscle fibers
  • Innervated by branches of Group Ib afferents
  • Contribute less to conscious sensation of muscle activity
  • Important role in reflex circuits protecting muscle from injury
Spindle vs GTO Arrangement: Muscle spindle = parallel with extrafusal fibers (senses length). Golgi tendon organ = in series with extrafusal fibers (senses tension).
Joint Receptors
  • Found in ligaments and joint capsules
  • Contribute to sensation of joint angle & position
  • Work alongside muscle spindles and Ruffini endings

Thalamus — Somatic Sensory Relay

Ventral Posterior (VP) Complex
  • Ascending somatic sensory pathways from spinal cord and brainstem converge here
  • Organization is highly organized
  • Contains a somatotopic representation of body & head
VP Lateral (VPL)
  • Relay from body
  • Via Medial Lemniscus
  • Somatotopic body map
VP Medial (VPM)
  • Relay from face/head
  • Via Trigeminal Lemniscus
  • Parallel pathway to body
Segregation of Information in Thalamus
  • Inputs carrying different types of somatosensory information terminate on separate populations of relay cells
  • Information from distinct receptor types (e.g., Merkel vs. Meissner) remains segregated in its passage to cortex
  • Also: Medial Thalamic and Parabrachial nuclei (for pain & affective touch) — project to anterior cingulate and insula
  • Most neurons from VP thalamus project to layer 4 of primary somatosensory cortex (SI)

Primary Somatosensory Cortex (SI) & Beyond

Location of SI
  • Postcentral gyrus of the parietal lobe
  • Has 4 distinct regions (Brodmann areas)
  • Each contains a complete somatotopic map of the body
  • Organized medial-to-lateral arrangement
Brodmann Area 3a
  • Responds primarily to proprioceptive stimulation
  • Input from Group Ia muscle spindle afferents
Brodmann Area 3b
  • Responds primarily to cutaneous stimulation
  • Obligatory first step in cortical processing
  • Receives largest VP thalamus input
  • Sends dense projections to areas 1 & 2
  • Lesion → deficits in ALL tactile sensations
Brodmann Areas 1 & 2
  • Area 1: cutaneous stimulation (texture discrimination)
  • Area 2: both tactile AND proprioceptive stimuli (size & shape)
  • Lesion area 1 → deficit in texture discrimination
  • Lesion area 2 → deficit in size and shape discrimination
Somatotopic Map — The Homunculus
  • Foot, leg, trunk, forelimbs, face in medial-to-lateral arrangement
  • Does NOT represent body in actual proportions
  • Bigger cortical area + more neurons for more richly innervated regions
  • Homunculus — grossly enlarged face & hands
  • Proportionality reflects neural circuitry required for associated functions
  • Examples: facial expressions, speech, manual manipulation of objects
  • Species-specific: rat = whiskers overrepresented; raccoon = paws; platypus = bill; naked mole rat = teeth dominate!
Columnar Organization in SI
  • Neurons form functionally distinct columns
  • RA and SA responses cluster into separate zones within single finger representation
  • Modular organization = fundamental feature of cortical organization
  • Functional significance still being determined

Plasticity in the Adult Cerebral Cortex

Lesion-Induced Plasticity
  • Lesioning a sensory input → initial lack of response in corresponding cortical area
  • Followed by gradual increase in responding to stimulation of neighboring regions
  • Adjacent body-part representations expand into deprived cortex
  • Example: digit 3 amputation in owl monkey → fingers 2 & 4 representation expands within 2 months
Experience-Induced Plasticity
  • Less drastic than lesion — changes via sensory or motor experience
  • Training a monkey to use specific fingers → expands associated cortical representation
  • Local anesthetic → temporary remapping of receptive fields
  • Rapid plasticity → likely reflects changes in synaptic strength of existing synapses (not new growth)
Musical Training & Plasticity
  • String players show increased cortical representation of left-hand fingers
  • Earlier onset of musical practice → greater cortical reorganization
  • Observed behaviorally, cortically (fMRI), and subcortically (EEG)
  • Key papers: Elbert et al. (1995) Science; Herholz & Zatorre (2012) Neuron
General Principle: Functional Remapping
  • Functional remapping is a general property of neocortex
  • Also observed in visual cortex, auditory cortex, and motor cortex
  • Cortical maps are dynamic, not fixed — they reflect current patterns of use
  • Has implications for rehabilitation, prosthetics, skill learning, and understanding phantom limb pain
Complete Pathway Summary — From Skin to Cortex
Stimulus → Receptor / Mechanoreceptor → Receptor Potential → Action Potentials → Peripheral Axon → DRG Cell Body → Central Axon → Dorsal Horn Synapse (Spinal Cord) → Dorsal Columns (ipsilateral ascent) → Dorsal Column Nuclei (Medulla) → Medial Lemniscus (crosses midline) → VP Thalamus (contralateral) → SI: Areas 3a, 3b, 1, 2 (postcentral gyrus) → SII → Amygdala / Hippocampus → Parietal areas 5 & 7 → Motor cortex
