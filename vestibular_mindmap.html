3 ‚Äî Vestibular Hair Cells (Mechanotransduction)

03
‚ö°
How Hair Cells Work
  • Transduces minute mechanical displacements into receptor potentials
  • Identical mechanism to auditory hair cells
  • Mechanically gated transduction channels at tips of stereocilia
  • Produces biphasic potentials (both depolarization and hyperpolarization)
‚Üë
Depolarization (Excitation)
  • Stereocilia deflect toward kinocilium
  • Mechanically gated channels open
  • K‚Å∫ flows in (from endolymph)
  • Cell depolarizes
  • Neurotransmitter released onto vestibular nerve fibers (CN VIII)
  • Increased firing of vestibular afferents
‚Üì
Hyperpolarization (Inhibition)
  • Stereocilia deflect away from kinocilium
  • Channels close
  • Cell hyperpolarizes
  • Reduced vestibular nerve activity
  • Below resting baseline firing rate
üß©
Hair Cell Structure
  • Kinocilium ‚Äî single tall cilium, defines polarity axis
  • Stereocilia ‚Äî graded height bundle; connected by tip links
  • Supporting cells ‚Äî structural support around hair cells
  • Nerve fibers ‚Äî afferent axons at base

4 ‚Äî Otolith Organs: Utricle & Saccule

04
üíé
Otolith Basics
  • Otolith = "ear stones"
  • Named for otoconia ‚Äî embedded crystals of calcium carbonate
  • Detect displacements and linear accelerations
  • Also sense static head position relative to gravitational axis (tilts)
üèó
Macula Structure (cross-section)
  • Macula = sensory epithelium in utricle & saccule
  • Hair bundles project into a gelatinous layer
  • Above: otolithic membrane (gelatinous)
  • Otoconia embedded in otolithic membrane (makes it heavier than surrounding fluid)
  • Reticular membrane, Supporting cells, Hair cells (base)
  • Striola ‚Äî dividing line visible at top
üìê
Mechanism: Static Head Tilt
1.Head tilts ‚Üí gravity acts on heavy otolithic membrane
2.Membrane shifts relative to sensory epithelium
3.Shearing motion between membrane and macula
4.Hair bundles displaced
5.Receptor potential generated
  • Response is tonic (sustained) as long as tilt is maintained
üöÄ
Mechanism: Linear Acceleration
1.Head undergoes linear acceleration
2.Greater mass of otolithic membrane causes it to lag behind the macula
3.Transient relative displacement of hair bundle
4.Transient receptor potential
  • Response is transient for translational movements (not sustained)
‚öñÔ∏è
Striola Division
  • Striola = dividing line in macula
  • Orientation of hair cells switches at striola
  • Tilt on striola axis: excites cells on one side, inhibits cells on other
‚ÜîÔ∏è
Utricle ‚Äî Horizontal Plane
  • Hair bundles oriented horizontally
  • Responds to translational movements in the horizontal plane
  • Detects sideways head tilts
  • Codes motion in horizontal plane
‚ÜïÔ∏è
Saccule ‚Äî Vertical Plane
  • Hair bundles oriented vertically
  • Responds to translational movements in the vertical plane
  • Detects upward/downward head tilts
  • Codes motion in vertical plane
üîÉ
Bilateral Mirroring
  • Saccular & utricular maculae are mirrored on both sides of the head
  • Tilting to one side has opposite effects on contralateral corresponding hair cells
  • Together, both sides provide complete 3D representation
üìà
Afferent Firing Properties (Otolith)
  • Afferents fire steadily when head is upright (baseline)
  • Tilt response: increase or decrease in firing depending on direction
  • Response is sustained as long as tilting force remains
  • Tonic response = head tilts ¬∑ Transient response = translational movements
üóÇ
Otolith Organ Recap
  • Utricle ‚Üí horizontal plane motion
  • Saccule ‚Üí vertical plane motion
  • Together ‚Üí respond to any linear force in 3D
  • Specific movements stimulate subsets of otolith hair cells while inhibiting others
  • Population-level changes in hair cell polarity comprehensively code head position & movement

5 ‚Äî Semicircular Canals

05
üåÄ
Anatomy of the Semicircular Canals
  • 3 canals sense head rotations from self-movement OR external angular accelerations
  • Crista = sensory epithelium at bulbous expansion (ampulla)
  • Hair bundles extend into gelatinous mass = cupula
  • Cupula bridges entire width of ampulla ‚Üí prevents endolymph circulation
  • All hair cells point in same direction (NO striola, no mirror axis)
  • Structures: Ampulla, Cupula, Crista, Nerve fibers, Hair cells
üéØ
Three Canal Planes & Axes
CanalAxisMotion type
HorizontalZ-axisPirouette (yaw)
Superior (anterior)Y-axisBackflip (pitch)
PosteriorX-axisCartwheel (roll)
  • Also named: +Z/LHRH, +LARP, +RALP axes in literature
üåä
Mechanism: Rotational Movements
1.Head turns on plane of one semicircular canal
2.Movement of endolymphatic fluid
3.Cupula distorted away from direction of head movement
4.Hair bundles displaced within the crista
5.Receptor potential generated
6.Neurotransmitter release ‚Üí vestibular nerve activated
‚ÜîÔ∏è
Mechanism: Translational Movements
  • Translational forces produce equal forces on both sides of the cupula
  • Hair bundles are NOT displaced
  • Therefore semicircular canals are insensitive to translational (linear) movements
  • This is why otolith organs are needed to detect linear motion
üîÅ
Push-Pull Bilateral Arrangement
  • Each canal works in concert with its partner on opposite side
  • Hair cells aligned oppositely ‚Üí opposite changes in firing rates
  • Activity increased on the side toward which head is moving
  • Example (head turns left): cupula pushed toward kinocilium on left ‚Üí left canal firing ‚Üë; same movement pushes cupula away on right ‚Üí right canal firing ‚Üì
  • 3 pairs: Horizontal, Superior/Posterior, Posterior/Superior
  • Encodes head rotation in any direction
üìä
Afferent Firing During Rotation
  • All canal afferents maintain high baseline firing rate
  • Information via increases AND decreases in firing rate
  • Acceleration: maximum increase in firing
  • Constant velocity: firing rates return to baseline
  • Deceleration: maximum inhibition (minimum firing)
  • Cupula can stay deflected for up to ~15 seconds at constant velocity
  • Prolonged acceleratory arcs (ships, planes, space, amusement parks) ‚Üí cupula undeflects while head still turning

7 ‚Äî Pathway ‚ë† : The Vestibulo-Ocular Reflex (VOR)

07
üëÄ
What the VOR Does
  • Produces eye movements that counter head movements
  • Permits gaze to remain fixed on a target despite head moving
  • Very fast: latency ~5 ms
  • Works in all planes of movement
üîÄ
VOR Circuit (Head Turns LEFT example)
1.Left horizontal canal activated ‚Üí left vestibular nucleus (medial + lateral)
2.Excitatory fibers from left medial vestibular nucleus cross midline to right abducens nucleus
3a.Abducens nucleus: motor pathway ‚Üí right lateral rectus contracts (right eye turns right)
3b.Excitatory projection crosses midline via MLF ‚Üí left oculomotor nucleus ‚Üí left medial rectus contracts (left eye turns right)
4.Inhibitory neurons from medial vestibular nucleus ‚Üí left abducens nucleus ‚Üí ‚Üì drive on left lateral rectus + ‚Üì drive on right medial rectus
5.Net: Both eyes turn right (opposite to head turn)
üé≠
Nystagmus
  • Physiological nystagmus: during horizontal head rotation, net differences in left vs. right vestibular nerve firing
  • Slow phase: eyes move counter to rotation direction (VOR compensatory)
  • Fast phase: eyes reset to new position (saccadic reset in same direction as rotation)
  • Spontaneous nystagmus: damage to one vestibular nerve ‚Üí eye movements in absence of head rotation (due to net difference in firing between two nerves)
‚ö†Ô∏è
Loss of VOR ‚Äî Consequences
  • Oscillopsia ("bouncing vision"): inability to fixate visual targets while head is moving
  • Unilateral damage: can be compensated over time
  • Bilateral damage: persistent sense that the world is moving when head moves; oculomotor centers receive no vestibular input ‚Üí no compensatory eye movements possible

9 ‚Äî Pathway ‚ë° : Descending (Spinal) Projections

09
ü¶í
Vestibulocervical Reflex (VCR)
  • Function: regulates head position via reflex activity of neck muscles
  • Stimulus: semicircular canal activation
  • Pathway: Semicircular canals ‚Üí Medial Vestibular Nucleus ‚Üí axons descend in Medial Longitudinal Fasciculus (MLF) ‚Üí upper cervical levels of spinal cord
  • Example: downward pitch (tripping) ‚Üí superior canals activated ‚Üí head muscles reflexively pull head up
ü¶µ
Vestibulospinal Reflex (VSR)
  • Function: mediates balance and upright posture
  • Stimulus: otolith organ activation
  • Pathway: Otolith organs ‚Üí Lateral Vestibular Nucleus (LVN) ‚Üí Lateral Vestibulospinal Tract ‚Üí ipsilateral ventral horn of spinal cord
  • Effect: activates extensor motor neurons + inhibits flexor motor neurons
  • Net: powerful excitatory influence on extensor (antigravity) muscles
  • Normally suppressed by cerebral cortex
‚ö°
Decerebrate Rigidity
  • Cutting brainstem above vestibular nuclei ‚Üí decerebrate rigidity (rigid extension of all limbs)
  • Lesioning the vestibular nuclei ‚Üí relieves decerebrate rigidity
  • Reveals role of vestibular system in maintaining muscle tone
  • Normally, cerebral cortex suppresses vestibulospinal drive
üó∫
Descending Tract Anatomy
  • Medial Vestibulospinal Tract ‚Üí bilateral, upper cord (head/neck)
  • Lateral Vestibulospinal Tract ‚Üí ipsilateral, entire cord (trunk/limb extensors)
  • Both originate from lateral vestibular nucleus + medial vestibular nucleus
  • Terminate in ventral horn ‚Üí alpha motor neurons

10 ‚Äî Vestibular-Cerebellar Pathways

10
üåø
Cerebellum & Vestibular System
  • Cerebellum is a major target of ascending vestibular pathways
  • Also sends descending projections back to vestibular nuclei
  • Important for integrating and modulating vestibular signals
  • Enables adaptive change in vestibular reflexes
üî¨
Purkinje Cell Roles
  • VOR adaptation: altered firing of Purkinje cells ‚Üí send inhibitory projections to vestibular nucleus ‚Üí modify VOR gain
  • Tilt vs. translation discrimination: Purkinje cells integrate signals from otolith organs + semicircular canals to differentiate head tilts from translational movements
  • Some cerebellar sub-regions do NOT respond to self-generated movement ‚Üí related to predictive/efference copy signals that cancel out vestibular/proprioceptive inputs
