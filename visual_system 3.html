eye movement
Saccadic Eye Movements
‚ö°
Rapid, ballistic jumps of the eye between fixation points
Used during normal scanning of scenes, reading
Form the fast phase component of nystagmus
Coordinated by the superior colliculus and frontal eye fields
eye movement
Smooth Pursuit Eye Movements
üîÑ
Slow, continuous eye movements that track a moving target
Require a moving target to initiate (cannot perform voluntarily on stationary target)
Form the slow phase component of nystagmus
Requires cortical input (posterior parietal cortex, MT)
reflex
Optokinetic Reflex (Optokinetic Nystagmus, OKN)
üåÄ
A gaze-stabilizing reflex triggered by large-field motion across the visual scene
Consists of two components:
Slow phase: smooth pursuit ‚Äî eyes track the moving scene
Fast phase: saccade ‚Äî eyes quickly reset to a new fixation point in the opposite direction
Neural pathway: a subset of directionally selective RGCs project to the Nucleus of the Optic Tract (NOT)
NOT activates cranial nerves controlling the eye muscles
Key evidence: blocking direction selectivity in the retina completely ablates the OKN (Yonehara et al., Neuron 2016)

Primary Visual Pathway
üåê
Underlies conscious vision
RGCs ‚Üí Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN) of the thalamus
LGN relay neurons ‚Üí Primary Visual Cortex (V1) via optic radiations
anatomy
Visual Field Segregation by Hemisphere
‚ÜîÔ∏è
Information from the right visual field ‚Üí left brain hemisphere
Information from the left visual field ‚Üí right brain hemisphere
Temporal retina signals from each eye join with nasal retina signals from the other eye at the optic chiasm
Each eye has its own visual field
Combined = binocular visual field
Strictly peripheral vision is monocular
optics
Eye Optics & Image Inversion
üî≠
Due to the optics of the eye, images entering the eye are flipped both vertically and horizontally
Superior visual field ‚Üí inferior retinal quadrant ‚Üí processed in superior V1
Inferior visual field ‚Üí superior retinal quadrant ‚Üí processed in inferior V1
Left visual field ‚Üí right temporal retina + left nasal retina
anatomy
LGN ‚Üí V1 Projections (Meyer's Loop)
üîÄ
Fibers representing superior visual fields (inferior retinal quadrants) travel dorsally from LGN
Fibers representing inferior visual fields (superior retinal quadrants) travel through Meyer's loop ventrally
These two fiber bundles take different anatomical paths from LGN to V1
Clinical implication: lesions at different points produce different visual field deficits

Hubel & Wiesel
Receptive Field Properties in V1
üìä
Classic experiments by David Hubel and Torsten Wiesel (Nobel Prize 1981)
Small circular spots that drove RGCs and LGN neurons did NOT strongly drive V1 cells
Orientation selective cells: prefer elongated edges at a specific angle
Direction selective cells: respond only when preferred-orientation bar moves in one of the angles perpendicular to the long-axis of the bar
Cells care about spatial frequency (size of stimulus) and temporal frequency (speed or flicker rate)
hierarchy
Hierarchical Organization: LGN ‚Üí V1
üìê
LGN neurons: center-surround receptive fields
V1 simple cells: orientation selective ‚Äî built by integrating inputs from a row of LGN neurons
Multiple aligned center-surround LGN neurons ‚Üí one orientation-selective cortical simple cell
Increasing complexity of receptive fields as you go higher in the visual hierarchy
columns
Functional Organization: Orientation Columns
üèõÔ∏è
Cells above and below each other (vertical): same visual field location, similar orientation selectivity = orientation column
Cells beside one another: less overlapping receptive fields, different preferred orientations
organization
Orientation Pinwheels
üåÄ
Surface view of V1: color-coded by preferred orientation of cells in each area
Similar orientations cluster together (orientation columns)
At any given receptive field location, all orientations are represented = orientation pinwheel
This pattern repeats at every new receptive field location
Pinwheels repeat every ~1 mm
Evidence from both macroscopic (optical imaging) and single-cell (electrode) recordings
binocularity
Binocular Vision Starts in V1
üëÄ
Contralateral and ipsilateral retinal axons project to separate layers in the LGN
Eye-specific segregation is maintained in layer 4 of V1 ‚Üí ocular dominance columns
Layer 4: robust ocular dominance (monocular responses)
Outside layer 4: most neurons are binocular (respond to both eyes)
But binocular cells generally receive stronger input from one eye (corresponding to their layer 4 column)
organization
Hypercolumn
üßä
All different spatially segregated functional modules overlay in V1
A hypercolumn contains:
One complete set of orientation columns (all 180¬∞)
Ocular dominance columns for both eyes
CO blobs (cytochrome oxidase patches ‚Äî color processing)
Interblob regions (form/orientation processing)
Each hypercolumn processes a single point in the visual field through all feature dimensions
depth perception
Stereopsis ‚Äî Depth from Disparity
üî≤
Stereopsis: sensation of depth from viewing nearby objects with two eyes in slightly different locations
Objects in front of or behind the fixation plane project to non-corresponding parts of both retinas
This binocular disparity generates the sensation of depth
V1 contains disparity-tuned cells:
Far cells: respond to objects behind fixation
Near cells: respond to objects in front of fixation
Tuned zero cells: respond to objects at the fixation plane
development
CO Blobs ‚Äì Color Vision Conduit
üé®
Cytochrome oxidase (CO) blobs: patches in layers 2/3 of V1 that stain darkly for CO
Receive input from koniocellular LGN layers
Thought to be a conduit for chromatic contrast (color) processing
Blobs in V1 ‚Üí thin stripes in V2 ‚Üí V4 "globs" (color processing cells)
Interblobs ‚Üí pale stripes in V2 ‚Üí involved in achromatic/form processing
Research by Beverly Conway on chromatic hierarchy
05 Disorders & Clinical Correlates
collapse ‚ñ≤
clinical
Strabismus and Amblyopia
ü©∫
Strabismus: misalignment of the eyes ‚Äî they don't properly align when looking at an object
Two common forms:
Esotropia: cross-eyed (eyes deviate inward)
Exotropia: eyes deviate outwards
Long-term, untreated strabismus in childhood ‚Üí amblyopia
Amblyopia ("lazy eye"): brain stops processing inputs from one eye; ocular dominance columns for that eye shrink
Critical period: occurs during visual development; treatment (patching strong eye) most effective early in life

üóÇÔ∏è

dorsal stream
Area MT & The Aperture Problem
üé•
MT (Middle Temporal area / V5): part of the dorsal stream; involved in detection of motion
V1 receptive fields are small ‚Äî may give misleading information on the direction of movement
This is called the aperture problem: a cell with a small receptive field can only see a portion of a moving edge ‚Äî cannot determine the true direction of the overall object's motion
Neurons in area MT integrate inputs from many smaller receptive field V1 neurons ‚Üí can detect the true overall direction of object movement

Face Cells ‚Äî High-Order Visual Object Selectivity
ü§ñ
Neurons in Area IT (Inferotemporal cortex) in macaques selectively respond to faces
Classic discovery: cells respond best to full face vs. caricature or scrambled lines
Removing eyes from a face reduces the response
Monkey Face Cells (Freiwald et al., 2009): respond to monkey and human faces; tuned to specific face angles (~80‚Äì100¬∞)
Do face cells really encode faces? (Ponce et al., 2019)
Used a deep generator network + genetic algorithm to evolve synthetic images that maximally drive face cells
Result: face cells drove strongly by face-like images ‚Äî but the "optimal" stimuli are not always perfectly face-like, suggesting the cells encode complex high-dimensional features associated with faces
