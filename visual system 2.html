The 'vertical' retinal pathway ‚Äî the primary route for visual signal transmission from light input to brain output.
üî¥

Photoreceptor ‚Üí Release

  • Upon absorbing photons, photoreceptors hyperpolarize
  • Hyperpolarization decreases glutamate release from axon terminal synapses
  • Glutamate is the neurotransmitter used
  • Photoreceptor is depolarized (and releases more glutamate) in the dark
‚ö°

Bipolar Cell ‚Üí Relay

  • Glutamate from photoreceptors binds to glutamate receptors on bipolar cell dendrites
  • When excited (depolarized), bipolar cells release glutamate from axon terminal synapses
  • Bodies reside in the inner nuclear layer (INL)
  • Axon terminals reside in the inner plexiform layer (IPL)
üì°

Ganglion Cell ‚Üí Output

  • Glutamate from bipolar cells binds to glutamate receptors on ganglion cell dendrites
  • Ganglion cells become depolarized (excited)
  • Fire action potentials that propagate out of the retina via the optic nerve
  • AMPA, kainate, and NMDA receptors present on ganglion cell dendrites
  • Glutamate acts as an excitatory transmitter here

ON & OFF Bipolar Cell Parallel Pathways

üåü

ON Bipolar Cells ‚Äî Mediate Brightening

  • Have metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGluR6) on their dendrites
  • Receptor is linked via an intracellular G-protein pathway to a Na‚Å∫ channel
  • Na‚Å∫ channel is closed when glutamate is bound
  • In the dark: glutamate bound ‚Üí Na‚Å∫ channel closed ‚Üí ON BC hyperpolarized (inactive)
  • In the light: less glutamate ‚Üí Na‚Å∫ channel opens ‚Üí ON BC depolarized (active)
  • Respond to light ON (brightening) of their receptive field center
  • Stratify in deeper layers of the IPL ‚Üí synapse onto ON ganglion cells

Dark (Light OFF)

Photoreceptor depolarized ‚Üí ‚Üë glutamate ‚Üí mGluR6 bound ‚Üí Na‚Å∫ channel closed ‚Üí ON BC hyperpolarized

Light ON

Photoreceptor hyperpolarized ‚Üí ‚Üì glutamate ‚Üí mGluR6 unbound ‚Üí Na‚Å∫ channel opens ‚Üí ON BC depolarized ‚Üí releases glutamate

üåë

OFF Bipolar Cells ‚Äî Mediate Dimming

  • Have ionotropic glutamate receptors (AMPA/kainate) on their dendrites
  • The receptor IS the ion channel ‚Äî same molecule
  • In the dark: glutamate bound ‚Üí channel opens ‚Üí OFF BC depolarized (active)
  • In the light: less glutamate ‚Üí channel closes ‚Üí OFF BC hyperpolarized (inactive)
  • Respond to light OFF (dimming) of their receptive field center
  • Stratify in upper layers of the IPL ‚Üí synapse onto OFF ganglion cells

Dark (Light OFF)

Photoreceptor depolarized ‚Üí ‚Üë glutamate ‚Üí ionotropic receptor opens ‚Üí OFF BC depolarized ‚Üí releases glutamate onto OFF GC

Light ON

Photoreceptor hyperpolarized ‚Üí ‚Üì glutamate ‚Üí ionotropic receptor closes ‚Üí OFF BC hyperpolarized

üß¨

Receptor Type Creates the Difference

  • Same photoreceptor glutamate signal ‚Üí opposite responses in ON vs OFF cells
  • Key distinction: ionotropic (OFF) vs metabotropic (ON)
  • This creates two parallel pathways that simultaneously encode brightness increases and decreases
mGluR6 ‚Üí ON AMPA/kainate ‚Üí OFF G-protein linked Na‚Å∫ channel

Retinal Layers & Bipolar Cell Stratification

üìê

Inner Nuclear Layer (INL)

  • Where bipolar cell bodies reside
  • Also contains horizontal cell and amacrine cell bodies
üìö

Inner Plexiform Layer (IPL) ‚Äî 5 Sublayers

  • Where bipolar cell axon terminals reside
  • Divided into sublayers 1‚Äì5 (numbered from outside to inside)
  • Sublayers 1‚Äì2 (upper/outer): OFF bipolar cells stratify here ‚Üí connect to OFF ganglion cells
  • Sublayers 4‚Äì5 (deeper/inner): ON bipolar cells stratify here ‚Üí connect to ON ganglion cells
  • Sublayer 3: border between ON and OFF sublaminae
  • ~12 types of cone bipolar cells exist, each stratifying at a distinct depth
Sublayers 1‚Äì2 = OFF Sublayers 4‚Äì5 = ON Stratification = function

ON/OFF Ganglion Cell Layering

  • ON and OFF ganglion cells project their dendritic arbors to different layers of the IPL
  • OFF ganglion cells receive input from OFF bipolar cells in upper IPL
  • ON ganglion cells receive input from ON bipolar cells in deeper IPL

Signal Coding & Light Adaptation

üìä

Intensity ‚Üí Action Potential Frequency

  • Light intensity is encoded as action potential frequency in retinal ganglion cells
  • High-intensity light ‚Üí more depolarization of bipolar ‚Üí higher firing rate of ganglion cell
  • Low-intensity light ‚Üí less depolarization ‚Üí lower firing rate
  • Ganglion cell firing rate is NOT an absolute measure of light intensity
üåó

Light Adaptation in the Retina

  • Ganglion cell firing rate is related to the difference between stimulus and background intensity
  • At a given background level, a spot's evoked response is proportional to its intensity over ~2 log units
  • Each background level shifts the response curve along the intensity axis
  • Allows us to see changes in light intensity across several log units of different background intensities
  • Critical for everyday vision (from dim starlight to bright sunlight)
Visual light range context
  • Scotopic (rod-mediated): starlight and below ‚Äî no colour, poor acuity
  • Mesopic: moonlight to indoor lighting ‚Äî transition between rod and cone vision
  • Photopic (cone-mediated): indoor lighting to sunlight ‚Äî good colour, best acuity
  • Cone threshold begins around ‚Äì4 log cd/m¬≤
  • Rod saturation begins around 0 log cd/m¬≤
  • Best acuity at about +2 log cd/m¬≤
  • Retinal damage possible at very high intensities
üí°

Luminance vs. Brightness

  • Luminance: physical measurement of light intensity (objective)
  • Brightness: the sensation elicited by light intensity (subjective)
  • The same luminance can have remarkably different perceived brightness depending on context
  • Context effects partly arise from lateral (surround) inhibition in the retina
  • Example: a gray square on black background looks brighter than the identical gray on white

Fovea vs. Periphery ‚Äî Visual Acuity

üéØ

The Fovea ‚Äî High Acuity Center

  • In the fovea, each cone forms the center of both an ON-center AND an OFF-center pathway
  • One-to-one (or near one-to-one) mapping: single cone ‚Üí dedicated bipolar cells ‚Üí dedicated ganglion cells
  • Results in small receptive fields = very high spatial acuity
  • Both an ON-centered and an OFF-centered ganglion cell are driven by each foveal cone
1 cone ‚Üí 1 ON GC + 1 OFF GC High acuity Small RF
üåê

Periphery ‚Äî Convergence

  • Outside the fovea, more photoreceptors feed into a single ganglion cell
  • Many-to-one convergence results in larger receptive fields
  • Larger receptive fields ‚Üí lower spatial acuity but greater light sensitivity
  • Receptive field size increases with eccentricity from fovea
Many cones ‚Üí 1 GC Large RF Low acuity, high sensitivity

Daytime (Cone) vs. Nighttime (Rod) Retinal Pathways

‚òÄÔ∏è

Cone (Daytime / Photopic) Pathway

  • Cones are active in daylight conditions
  • ON and OFF cone bipolar cells directly synapse with ganglion cells
  • Direct, fast pathway with no intermediate AII amacrine step
  • Supports good colour vision and best spatial acuity
üåô

Rod (Nighttime / Scotopic) Pathway

1

Rods are active in nighttime conditions

2

Rods connect to a dedicated rod bipolar cell ‚Äî which is an ON-type bipolar cell

3

Rod bipolar cells do NOT directly synapse onto ganglion cells

4

Instead, rod bipolar cells make glutamatergic synapses with AII ("A2") amacrine cells

5

AII amacrine cells form electrical synapses (gap junctions) with ON cone bipolar cells

6

Rod signal then travels via cone bipolar ‚Üí ganglion cell. Rod signals "piggy-back" onto the cone bipolar system to reach ganglion cells

‚öñÔ∏è

Key Differences

Cone Pathway

Cone ‚Üí Cone BC (ON/OFF) ‚Üí Ganglion cell

Direct synapse, no AII step

Rod Pathway

Rod ‚Üí Rod BC (ON only) ‚Üí AII Amacrine ‚Üí ON Cone BC ‚Üí Ganglion cell

Indirect, via gap junction

Rod and cone signals take different pathways through the retina. AII amacrine cells are the critical bridge for rod signals.

Retinal Ganglion Cell Diversity & Visual Feature Extraction

üé®

The Retina Extracts Multiple Visual Features

  • Different ganglion cells extract unique visual features
  • Examples: edges, motion, colour, orientation, direction, contrast
  • Each ganglion cell type "tiles" the retina ‚Äî providing complete coverage of the visual field
  • Think of each ganglion cell type as a different type of pixel-sensor in a digital camera
  • Within a given pixel area (part of visual field), each type of pixel sensor is present
  • Ganglion cell types receive inputs from a specific microcircuit of specific bipolar and amacrine cells
  • These microcircuits also repeat across the retina along with their ganglion cell type
da Silveira and Roska, 2011 ‚Äî repeating circuit modules tile the retina
üê≠

Mouse Retina ‚Äî Functional Diversity

  • "Functional phenotyping" reveals over 40 distinct functional cell types in mouse retina (Baden et al., Nature, 2016)
  • Responses compared across a series of different visual stimuli
  • Stimuli used include: chirp, moving bars, noise, colour, etc.
  • Cell types include: OFF local, OFF DS, OFF step, OFF slow, OFF alpha, ON-OFF local, ON DS, ON alpha, ON low freq, ON sust., etc.
Baden et al. (Nature, 2016)

Center-Surround Receptive Fields

üîµ

What Is a Receptive Field?

  • A receptive field for a visual neuron is an area in space in which light stimulation drives a neuronal response
  • Ganglion cells are not good at responding to diffuse illumination
  • Instead, they respond well to luminance contrast within their receptive field
  • Each ganglion cell has a preferred size visual input ‚Äî usually a spot matching its receptive field center
‚äï

ON-Center Ganglion Cells

  • Excited by light in their receptive field center
  • Inhibited by light in their receptive field surround
  • Respond well to a bright spot on a dark background matching center size
  • Project dendrites to deeper layers (ON sublayer) of the IPL
  • Receive excitatory input from ON bipolar cells
Center: excite (+) Surround: inhibit (‚àí)
‚äñ

OFF-Center Ganglion Cells

  • Inhibited by light in their receptive field center
  • Excited by light in their receptive field surround
  • Respond well to a dark spot on a bright background matching center size
  • Project dendrites to upper layers (OFF sublayer) of the IPL
  • Receive excitatory input from OFF bipolar cells
Center: inhibit (‚àí) Surround: excite (+)
üìà

Receptive Field Response Properties

  • The same stimulus ‚Üí remarkably different responses depending on which part of the RF it falls on
  • Optimal response: stimulus spot matches exactly the RF center size
  • As spot grows into the surround, response decreases (surround suppression)
  • Diffuse full-field illumination gives a weak response because center and surround oppose each other
  • Center-surround structure enables contrast detection and edge detection

Lateral Inhibition ‚Äî Horizontal & Amacrine Cells

üîÑ

Horizontal Cells ‚Äî Surround Inhibition at the Outer Retina

1

Light activates center cone ‚Üí photoreceptor hyperpolarizes ‚Üí reduces glutamate release from center photoreceptor

2

Simultaneously, light in the surround also activates surround photoreceptors ‚Üí they reduce glutamate release

3

Horizontal cells receive glutamate from photoreceptors. Less glutamate from surround ‚Üí horizontal cells hyperpolarize

4

Horizontal cell hyperpolarization negatively feeds back to the center photoreceptor

5

This feedback causes the center photoreceptor to slightly depolarize (counteracting its own hyperpolarization)

6

Net effect: the center cone's response is weakened when the surround is also illuminated ‚Üí surround inhibition!

7

This feedback can get passed downstream to retinal ganglion cells via bipolar cells

Negative feedback to photoreceptors Outer plexiform layer (OPL) Gap junctions between horizontal cells
üö´

Amacrine Cells ‚Äî Surround Inhibition at the Inner Retina

  • Amacrine cells provide lateral inhibition to bipolar cell terminals and ganglion cell dendrites
  • When surround bipolar cells are activated, they excite amacrine cells
  • Amacrine cells then inhibit the center bipolar cell axon terminal and/or the ganglion cell directly
  • Adding a surround stimulus reduces the ganglion cell response to the center
  • Many different amacrine cell types ‚Äî ~30 types in mammals
  • Also involved in direction selectivity (see Section 10)
Lateral inhibition in IPL Inner plexiform layer Inhibitory (GABA / glycine)

Direction Selectivity

‚û°Ô∏è

Direction Selective (DS) Ganglion Cells

  • Some ganglion cells are sensitive to the direction of motion of a moving image
  • Fire strongly when a stimulus moves in the preferred direction
  • Fire weakly (or not at all) when a stimulus moves in the null direction (opposite)
  • In rabbit retina, DS cells have 4 preferred directions: superior, inferior, anterior, posterior (Oyster & Barlow, Science, 1967)
  • Nearly 60 years after rabbit, DS ganglion cells finally found in primate retina (Kim et al., Nature Communications, 2022)
  • Primate DS cells show bistratified morphology (outer + inner dendrites spanning both ON and OFF sublayers)
  • Form a regular mosaic (coverage ~1)
‚≠ê

Mechanism: Starburst Amacrine Cells & Asymmetric Inhibition

  • DS ganglion cells receive inhibitory input from starburst amacrine cells
  • Starburst amacrine cells are connected to only one side of the DS ganglion cell's dendritic/receptive field
  • This is an asymmetric arrangement

Preferred Direction ‚Üí

Stimulus reaches ganglion cell FIRST ‚Üí ganglion cell is excited before starburst amacrine activates. Starburst arrives too late to block excitation. ‚Üí Ganglion cell fires

‚Üê Null Direction

Stimulus reaches starburst amacrine FIRST ‚Üí starburst activates and sends inhibition to ganglion cell. Then bipolar cell excitation arrives but is blocked. ‚Üí Ganglion cell silent

Demb, 2007 ‚Äî Asymmetric lateral inhibition as the mechanistic basis for direction selectivity
üëÅÔ∏è

Optokinetic Reflex

  • The optokinetic reflex stabilizes moving scenes on the eye
  • DS retinal ganglion cells sensitive to the direction of visual flow are required for this behaviour
  • Mice lacking DS ganglion cells show impaired optokinetic reflexes
Yonehara et al. (Neuron, 2016)

Colour Vision & Colour Opponency

üåà

Colour Opponency

  • Retinal ganglion cells encode colour through opponent mechanisms
  • A red-ON / green-OFF cell: increases firing to red light covering its RF; decreases firing to green light
  • Opponent pairs: Red vs. Green and Blue vs. Yellow
  • These are the four "psychological primaries" ‚Äî you cannot perceive a "reddish-green" or "bluish-yellow"

Red/Green Opponency

Mediated by Midget (Parvocellular) ganglion cells. Red-ON center, Green-OFF surround (or vice versa)

Blue/Yellow Opponency

Mediated by Bistratified (Koniocellular) ganglion cells. Blue-ON center, Yellow-OFF surround

üî¨

Midget (Parvocellular) Cells ‚Äî Colour & Acuity

  • Red/Green colour opponent
  • Small receptive fields ‚Üí high spatial acuity
  • ON-center and OFF-center variants exist
  • Most numerous cell type in primate retina
  • Project to parvocellular (P) layers of the LGN
üåä

Parasol (Magnocellular) Cells ‚Äî Motion & Luminance

  • Encode luminance (brightness), not colour
  • Large receptive fields ‚Üí motion detection
  • ON-center and OFF-center variants
  • Project to magnocellular (M) layers of the LGN
  • Faster temporal responses than midget cells
üíô

Bistratified (Koniocellular) Cells ‚Äî Blue/Yellow

  • Blue/Yellow colour opponent
  • Blue-ON, Yellow-OFF (or vice versa)
  • Dendrites stratify in both ON and OFF layers of the IPL (bistratified)
  • Project to koniocellular (K) layers of the LGN
