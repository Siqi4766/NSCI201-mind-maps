What is Sound?
Physical Nature of Sound
  • Pressure waves generated by vibrating air molecules
  • Analogous to ripples spreading across water ‚Äî but in 3D space
  • Displacements of air molecules:
  • Condensation ‚Äî molecules pressed together (higher pressure)
  • Rarefaction ‚Äî molecules spread apart (lower pressure)
  • Forms a sinusoidal wave around normal atmospheric pressure
  • Travels as concentric waves radiating outward from source
4 Features of Sound
  • Amplitude dB ‚Äî correlates with perceived loudness
  • Frequency Hz ‚Äî correlates with perceived pitch
  • Waveform ‚Äî amplitude plotted across time
  • Phase ‚Äî the position in the cycle at a given moment; described in degrees (Œ∏)
Complex Sounds
  • Birdsong & speech contain highly periodic elements
  • Can be modeled as the sum of sinusoidal waves of varying amplitudes, frequencies & phases
  • Fourier Transform ‚Äî decomposes a time function into constituent frequencies
  • Analogous to what the inner ear does mechanically
  • Environmental sounds (e.g. wind) lack periodic structure

The Audible Spectrum
  • Hearing range is species-specific
  • Species emphasize frequencies of their own vocalizations
  • Also shaped by ecology: echolocation (high freq), predation (low freq)
  • Humans: 20 Hz ‚Äì 20 kHz
  • Adults lose high frequencies ‚Üí max ~15‚Äì17 kHz

  • Bat: 60‚Äì130k Hz

The External Ear
Anatomy
  • Pinna ‚Äî outer visible cartilaginous flap
  • Concha ‚Äî bowl-shaped depression leading to canal
  • Auditory meatus (canal) ‚Äî tube channeling sound
  • All structures funnel sound energy to the tympanic membrane (eardrum)
Resonance & Amplification
  • Configuration of human auditory meatus selectively boosts sound pressure
  • Boost: 30‚Äì100√ó for frequencies around ~3 kHz
  • 3 kHz is directly related to speech perception
Elevation Cues
  • Pinna & concha filter different sound frequencies
  • This spectral filtering provides cues about the elevation of the sound source
  • Allows localization in the vertical plane
  • Processed in the dorsal cochlear nucleus

The Middle Ear
Impedance Matching
  • Middle ear boosts sound pressure from tympanic membrane ‚Üí inner ear by ~200√ó
  • Necessary to bridge air (low impedance) ‚Üí aqueous inner ear (high impedance)
  • Impedance = a medium's resistance to movement
  • Without this, ~99.9% of sound energy would be reflected
Two Mechanical Pressure Boosters
  • 1. Area ratio: Force focused from large tympanic membrane onto small-diameter oval window
  • 2. Lever action of the ossicles
  • Ossicles = Malleus, Incus, Stapes (smallest bones in body)
  • Malleus connects to tympanic membrane; stapes connects to oval window
Protective Muscles
  • Tensor tympani ‚Äî innervated by CN V
  • Stapedius ‚Äî innervated by CN VII
  • In response to loud noise, these muscles contract to limit ossicle movement and reduce transmission
  • Bell's palsy ‚Äî paralysis of these muscles ‚Üí hyperacusis (painful sensitivity to sound)

The Inner Ear & Cochlea
Cochlea Structure
  • Small coiled tube resembling a snail shell
  • Oval window & round window at the basal end
  • Stapes sits in oval window, transmitting vibrations
  • Two functions: sensory transduction + mechanical frequency analysis
  • Transforms pressure waves ‚Üí neural impulses in auditory nerve
3 Fluid Chambers (Scalae)
  • Scala vestibuli ‚Äî above cochlear partition; filled with perilymph
  • Scala tympani ‚Äî below cochlear partition; filled with perilymph
  • Scala media (cochlear duct) ‚Äî runs within the partition; filled with endolymph
  • Scala vestibuli & scala tympani connect at the helicotrema at the apex
  • Perilymph = low K‚Å∫, high Na‚Å∫ (like other body fluids)
Cochlear Partition
  • Flexible structure running from base almost to apex
  • Supports the basilar membrane (below) & tectorial membrane (above)
  • Spiral ganglion ‚Äî cell bodies of CN VIII neurons, located alongside cochlea
  • Inner & outer hair cells sit on basilar membrane
Tonotopy ‚Äî Basilar Membrane
  • Stiffness of basilar membrane varies along its length ‚Üí differential frequency sensitivity
  • Base (stiff) ‚Üí tuned to HIGH frequencies up to 20,000 Hz
  • Apex (flexible) ‚Üí tuned to LOW frequencies down to 25 Hz
  • Travelling wave propagates from base ‚Üí apex, growing in amplitude until maximum displacement at the characteristic frequency location
  • This mechanical decomposition = the ear's Fourier transform analogue

Sensory Transduction ‚Äî Organ of Corti
Hair Cell Location & Types
  • Hair cells located between basilar & tectorial membranes
  • Sound ‚Üí vibration ‚Üí shearing motion between the two membranes
  • Inner hair cells (IHC): true sensory receptors
  • Receive afferent innervation from CN VIII ‚Üí carry impulses toward CNS
  • ~3,500 IHCs in humans
  • Outer hair cells (OHC): amplifiers
  • Receive mostly efferent innervation
  • Produce otoacoustic emissions
  • ~12,000 OHCs in humans
Stereocilia & Tip-Links
  • Hair cells = epithelial cells with a bundle of hair-like processes protruding into scala media
  • Each bundle has 30 ‚Äì hundreds of stereocilia, graded in height
  • Tallest = kinocilium
  • Tip-links ‚Äî connect tips of adjacent stereocilia
  • Translate hair bundle movement into receptor potential
  • Essentially the "gate" of the mechanosensory channel
Transduction Steps
  • Step 1: Bundle deflects toward tallest stereocilia (kinocilium) ‚Üí tip-links stretch
  • Step 2: Tip-links directly open cation-selective channels (K‚Å∫ entry)
  • Step 3: K‚Å∫ influx ‚Üí depolarization of hair cell
  • Step 4: Depolarization opens voltage-gated Ca¬≤‚Å∫ channels
  • Step 5: Ca¬≤‚Å∫ influx ‚Üí neurotransmitter release onto auditory nerve endings
  • Reverse: Opposite deflection compresses tip-links ‚Üí channels close ‚Üí hyperpolarization
  • Some channels open at rest ‚Üí receptor potential is biphasic
Transduction Speed & Damage
  • Hair cell transduction is fast & sensitive ‚Äî ~10 Œºs
  • Speed critical for sound localization
  • Mechanical gating of ion channels enables this rapid response (vs. slow second-messenger pathways)
  • Damage to stereocilia (e.g. loud sounds) ‚Üí irreversible hearing loss
  • Damage to tip-links ‚Üí temporary hearing loss only (tip-links regenerate within hours)
Frequency Following
  • At low frequencies (<3 kHz): hair cell produces a sinusoidal receptor potential faithfully tracking stimulation
  • At higher frequencies: hair cell responds with a DC offset only ‚Äî does NOT faithfully track the frequency
  • AC component visible at 300‚Äì1,000 Hz
  • DC only at 2,000‚Äì5,000 Hz
  • Hair cells release neurotransmitter only when depolarized
  • ‚Üí Auditory nerve fibers fire only in the rising phase of low-frequency sounds

Ionic Basis of Mechanotransduction
Endolymph vs. Perilymph
  • Basal & apical surfaces separated by tight junctions ‚Üí separate ionic environments
  • Endolymph (scala media / apical): rich in K‚Å∫, poor in Na‚Å∫ +80 mV
  • Produced by ion pumping in stria vascularis
  • Perilymph (scala tympani / basal): poor in K‚Å∫, rich in Na‚Å∫ 0 mV
  • Similar to other body fluids
  • Endocochlear potential = difference between endolymph & perilymph
  • Hair cell interior: ‚àí45 mV vs. perilymph; ~‚àí125 mV vs. endolymph
K‚Å∫ Recycling Mechanism
  • Large electrical gradient drives K‚Å∫ into stereocilia from endolymph
  • Depolarization opens somatic K‚Å∫ & Ca¬≤‚Å∫ channels
  • Somatic K‚Å∫ channels ‚Üí K‚Å∫ efflux toward perilymph (repolarization)
  • Perilymph is low in K‚Å∫ ‚Üí favorable gradient for K‚Å∫ exit
  • Result: extremely fast and energy-efficient repolarization
  • Ionic gradient maintained even during prolonged stimulation
  • Avoids need for Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫-ATPase at every cycle

Auditory Nerve ‚Äî Tuning & Timing
Phase-Locking
  • Hair cell potential is biphasic but transmitter released only during depolarization
  • ‚Üí Auditory nerve fibers fire only in the rising phase of low-frequency sounds <3 kHz
  • This creates phase-locking
  • Phase-locking provides temporal information
  • Temporal info compared between the two ears ‚Üí sound localization
  • Required for ITD-based localization
Tuning Curve & Characteristic Frequency
  • Tuning curve ‚Äî plots minimum sound level to increase fiber's firing rate above baseline (threshold)
  • Characteristic frequency (CF) ‚Äî frequency at which fiber responds to the weakest stimulus
  • Each fiber has a sharply tuned CF corresponding to hair cell location
  • Tuning curves are V-shaped with a sharp tip at CF
Labeled-Line Coding
  • Provided by tonotopic organization of basilar membrane
  • Hair cell location along basilar membrane ‚Üí maximally responsive frequency
  • Each auditory fiber innervates only one IHC ‚Üí transmits only a narrow frequency band
  • Topographic organization retained throughout the ascending auditory system
  • Frequency information thus preserved at every relay
09
Sound Localization
Multiple Mechanisms Summary
Horizontal Plane
  • ITD ‚Äî Interaural Time Difference ‚Üí low freq (<3 kHz) ‚Üí processed in MSO
  • IID ‚Äî Interaural Intensity Difference ‚Üí high freq (>2 kHz) ‚Üí processed in LSO
Vertical Plane (Elevation)
  • Spectral filtering by pinna & concha
  • Processed in the dorsal cochlear nucleus
ITD ‚Äî Interaural Time Difference
  • Produced by difference in arrival time based on sound source position relative to each ear
  • Maximum ITD: ~700 Œºs
  • Humans can detect ITDs as small as 10 Œºs
  • Artificial manipulation of ITDs creates perception of sound from leading ear's side
  • Requires phase-locking ‚Üí only works at <3 kHz
Bilateral anteroventral cochlear nuclei ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚ñ∂ MSO (Medial Superior Olive) ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚ñ∂ Inferior Colliculus
MSO ‚Äî Coincidence Detection
  • MSO neurons have bipolar dendrites:
  • Lateral dendrites ‚Üí ipsilateral input
  • Medial dendrites ‚Üí contralateral input
  • Neurons function as coincidence detectors ‚Äî fire maximally when BOTH inputs arrive simultaneously
  • Different neurons sensitive to different ITDs
  • Axon lengths from anteroventral cochlear nucleus systematically vary to create intrinsic delays
  • Variation creates a map of horizontal sound location:
  • Neuron E ‚Üí sounds on LEFT
  • Neuron A ‚Üí sounds on RIGHT
  • Neuron C ‚Üí sounds at MIDLINE
IID ‚Äî Interaural Intensity Difference
  • At frequencies >2 kHz, head acts as acoustic obstacle (wavelengths too short to bend around it)
  • High-frequency sound directed to one side creates an acoustic shadow at the far ear
  • Intensity difference provides localization cue
  • Does NOT require phase-locking
LSO & MNTB ‚Äî IID Computation
  • LSO (Lateral Superior Olive) + MNTB compute sound position using IID
  • LSO receives direct excitatory input from ipsilateral cochlear nucleus
  • Contralateral inputs arrive via inhibitory interneurons in MNTB
  • LSO neurons fire most strongly when sound is directly lateral (ipsilateral side)
  • Ipsilateral excitation strong + contralateral inhibition weak
  • For contralateral sounds: inhibition > excitation ‚Üí net inhibition ‚Üí no signal
  • Result: graded representation of sound laterality

Ascending Auditory Pathway ‚Äî Brainstem to Thalamus
Cochlear Nucleus (1st Relay)
  • CN VIII enters brainstem ‚Üí branches to all 3 divisions of cochlear nucleus
  • Anteroventral cochlear nucleus
  • Posteroventral cochlear nucleus
  • Dorsal cochlear nucleus
  • Tonotopic organization of cochlea maintained in all subdivisions
  • Cell bodies of CN VIII ‚Üí spiral (cochlear) ganglion
  • High degree of bilateral connectivity
  • Bilateral connectivity supports binaural processing & localization
Superior Olive Complex (Pons)
  • Receives input from both cochlear nuclei
  • MSO ‚Äî computes ITD (low frequency)
  • LSO ‚Äî computes IID (high frequency)
  • MNTB ‚Äî inhibitory relay for IID computation
Monaural Pathways
  • From cochlear nucleus ‚Üí bypass superior olive
  • Terminate in nuclei of the lateral lemniscus on contralateral brainstem
  • Respond only to sound from one ear
  • Cells respond to specific properties:
  • Onset of sound
  • Duration of sound
Inferior Colliculus (Midbrain)
  • Major auditory midbrain integration center
  • Receives input from all ascending pathways: LSO, MSO, lateral lemniscus, cochlear nucleus (direct)
  • Converging info: timing, intensity, frequency
  • Brainstem inputs computed into topographical representation of auditory space
  • Neurons respond best to sounds from specific region of space
  • Also respond to complex temporal patterns ‚Äî preferences for frequency or duration
Auditory Thalamus (MGC)
  • All ascending auditory information must pass through the Medial Geniculate Complex (MGC)
  • Tonotopic inputs from brainstem converge onto MGC neurons
  • Can generate selective responses to combinations of frequencies or specific time intervals between frequencies
  • Ventral MGC ‚Üí primary auditory cortex
  • Dorsal MGC ‚Üí belt & parabelt (secondary auditory cortex)
Cochlea ‚Üí Spiral Ganglion ‚Üí CN VIII ‚Üí Cochlear Nucleus (medulla) ‚Üí Superior Olive (pons) ‚Üí Nucleus Lat. Lemniscus ‚Üí Inferior Colliculus (midbrain) ‚Üí MGC Thalamus ‚Üí Auditory Cortex

Auditory Cortex
Organization
  • Major target of ascending fibers from MGC
  • Two main subdivisions:
  • Primary auditory cortex (core):
  • Point-to-point input from ventral MGC
  • Contains precise tonotopic map
  • Belt & Parabelt (secondary):
  • More diffuse input from dorsal MGC
  • Also receives from primary auditory cortex
  • Less precise tonotopic organization
  • Strongly reciprocally interconnected (belt ‚Üî core)
Tonotopic Maps
  • Auditory cortex contains topographic maps of the cochlea
  • Because cochlear frequencies are tonotopically arranged ‚Üí cortical maps = tonotopic maps
  • Primary cortex ‚Äî precise frequency representation
  • Secondary cortex ‚Äî less precise, more integrative
Binaural Organization
  • Irregular patches of neurons arranged in alternating stripes:
  • EE cells ‚Äî excited by input from both ears
  • EI cells ‚Äî excited by one ear, inhibited by the other
  • Precise function of this organization is still unknown
Pitch & Temporal Processing
  • Secondary auditory regions important for pitch perception
  • Pitch perception fundamental to music & vocal communication
  • Allows differentiation of overlapping sounds
  • Important for temporal characteristics of auditory percepts
  • Critical for speech perception (highly ordered temporal structure)
  • Some neurons respond to temporal order of species-specific sounds
  • Unresponsive to same sound played in reverse
Clinical & Functional Notes
  • Bilateral damage ‚Üí severe problems processing temporal order; difficulty with music
  • Integrates non-auditory info from cortical & subcortical regions
  • Activated by motor gestures (vocal & manual ‚Äî esp. in musicians)
  • Wernicke's area (language comprehension) ‚Äî continuous with secondary auditory cortex
  • Auditory cortex activity diminishes just before a vocalization ‚Äî filters anticipated acoustical movements
Lateralization
  • Speech ‚Üí activates LEFT auditory cortex more strongly
  • Music ‚Üí activates RIGHT auditory cortex more strongly
  • Environmental sounds ‚Üí bilateral but right-dominant
  • Significant inter-individual differences in lateralization
  • Pattern holds in both core and belt regions
