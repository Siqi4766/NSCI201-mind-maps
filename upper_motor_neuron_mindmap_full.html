Motor System Architecture & LMN Review
4 Motor Systems
  • Motor cortex ‚Äî planning, initiating & directing voluntary movements
  • Basal ganglia ‚Äî initiation of intended movement; suppression of unwanted movement
  • Brainstem centers ‚Äî rhythmic, stereotyped movements & postural control
  • Cerebellum ‚Äî coordination of ongoing movement
Lower Motor Neurons ‚Äî Final Common Pathway
  • Convey motor commands to muscles
  • Each LMN innervates muscle fibers in one muscle = motor neuron pool
  • Somatotopy in ventral horn: medial = proximal; lateral = distal
Œ± vs Œ≥ Motor Neurons
  • Œ±-MNs ‚Üí extrafusal fibers ‚Üí force generation (motor unit)
  • Œ≥-MNs ‚Üí intrafusal (spindle) fibers ‚Üí regulate stretch reflex gain ‚Üí set muscle tone
Negative Feedback Systems
  • Muscle spindles (Ia & II afferents) ‚Üí monitor & maintain muscle length
  • Golgi tendon organs (Ib afferents) ‚Üí monitor & maintain muscle force
LMNs integrate all descending commands + sensory input ‚Äî they are the ONLY output to skeletal muscle.

Voluntary Movement vs Reflex
Key Differences
  • Voluntary = organized around a purposeful task
  • Reflexes = 1-to-1 stimulus ‚Üí response mapping
  • Voluntary: multiple neurons of many brain regions coordinate muscles toward a goal
  • Voluntary: effectiveness improves with purposeful practice
  • Voluntary: internally generated ‚Äî not necessarily responses to environmental stimuli

Primary Motor Cortex (M1)
Location & Properties
  • Located in the precentral gyrus (Area 4)
  • Low current threshold ‚Üí elicits movement & muscle contractions
  • Low threshold = large, direct pathway to lower MNs of brainstem & spinal cord
  • Receives input from basal ganglia & cerebellum via thalamus
  • Upper MNs mediate planning and initiation of movements
Cell Types (Cytoarchitecture)
  • Upper MNs are pyramidal cells
  • Betz cells: largest cell soma in CNS; only in M1; 5% of projections to spinal cord; important for distal muscle control; located in layer V
  • Non-Betz pyramidal cells: found in ALL divisions of motor & premotor cortex
Motor Homunculus (Penfield Map)
  • Medial ‚Üí trunk, lower extremity
  • Lateral ‚Üí face, tongue, lips, jaw, vocalization, swallowing
  • Corticospinal tract: trunk, upper & lower extremities
  • Corticobulbar tract: face
  • Hand & face disproportionately large (fine motor demands)
Action goals for personal space ¬∑ Direction selectivity at population level

Historical Experimental Evidence ‚Äî Functional Organisation of Motor Cortex
Fritsch & Hitzig (1870s)
  • Electrical stimulation of motor cortex
  • ‚Üí muscle contractions on the far (contralateral) side of the body
Hughlings Jackson
  • Motor cortex has representation of musculature
  • Epileptic partial seizures "march" systematically:
  • Finger ‚Üí hand ‚Üí forearm ‚Üí arm ‚Üí shoulder ‚Üí face
  • = Jacksonian march
Wilder Penfield ‚Äî "Montreal Procedure"
  • Patients remain awake during surgery
  • Describe reactions while surgeon stimulates brain areas
  • Mapped precentral gyrus in 400+ neurosurgical patients
  • Correlated location of muscle contractions with stimulation site
  • Demonstrated human motor cortex has map of musculature
Intracortical Microstimulation (1960s)
  • Upper MNs in layer V stimulated focally
  • Small currents ‚Üí excitation of several muscles
  • Movements elicited from widely separated stimulation sites
  • ‚Üí local circuits involved in controlling movements
  • ‚Üí movements, not individual muscles, might be organised
Evarts (1960s) ‚Äî Intracortical Recordings
  • Developed system for recording from motor cortex neurons
  • Firing rate ‚àù force: higher force ‚Üí higher firing frequency
  • Firing rates increase prior to force development
  • ‚Üí motor cortex contributes to early phase of movement generation & planning
Spike-Triggered Averaging (1970s)
  • Correlates activity of a single cortical neuron with muscle EMG
  • Task: wrist flexion or extension
  • Shows a peripheral muscle group activated by one motoneuron
  • Single upper MN contacts several lower MN pools
  • "Postspike facilitation" detected (n = 9000 spikes averaged)
  • ‚Üí consistent with cortical neurons encoding movements not muscles
Graziano (2005) ‚Äî Long Microstimulation
  • Awake behaving monkeys; longer stimulations (several seconds)
  • Elicits coordinated, purposeful, multi-joint movements
  • Movement types mapped: hand-to-mouth, defensive, central space/manipulation, reach, outward arm movements, climbing/leaping
  • Same stimulation site ‚Üí hand always moves to same final position regardless of starting position (red dots = final positions)
  • ‚Üí Action maps: movements can be elicited from widely separated motor cortex neurons
  • Question raised: map of musculature, movements, or intention of movements?

Corticospinal Tract (CST) & Corticobulbar Tract (CBT)
Shared Pathway
Motor Cortex ‚Üì Internal Capsule ‚Üì Ventral surface of midbrain ‚Üì Cerebral Peduncle ‚Üì Pons ‚Üì Ventral surface of Medulla ‚Üì Spinal Cord
CST ‚Äî Two Divisions
  • Lateral CST (90%): decussate at caudal medulla (pyramidal decussation) ‚Üí contralateral; terminates lateral ventral horn & IZ; most axons ‚Üí local circuit neurons; some directly onto Œ±-MNs ‚Üí distal limbs, forearm, hand ‚Üí fine motor: writing, piano, buttoning
  • Ventral CST (10%): do NOT cross; continue ipsilaterally; terminate bilaterally; arises from dorsal & medial regions of motor cortex; serves axial & proximal muscles
Corticobulbar Tract (CBT)
  • Branches off in brainstem
  • Gives rise to bilateral collaterals ‚Üí multiple brainstem nuclei
  • Mostly terminate on local circuit neurons
  • Controls muscles of head, face & neck
  • Functions: facial expressions, chewing, tongue movements
Brainstem Nuclei Innervated (CBT)
Trigeminal nucleus (V) ‚Äî chewing Facial motor nucleus (VII) ‚Äî facial expression Hypoglossal nucleus (XII) ‚Äî tongue Pontine fiber bundles Red nucleus
Termination Targets of Lateral CST
  • Lateral ventral horn ‚Üí distal limb LMNs
  • Intermediate zone ‚Üí local circuit neurons
  • Direct monosynaptic onto Œ±-MNs (unique to primates; allows fine motor control)
Key rule: Brainstem ‚Üí medial spinal cord (bilateral) ‚Üí posture/balance. Cortex ‚Üí lateral spinal cord (contralateral) ‚Üí skilled/voluntary.

Premotor Cortex & Mirror Neurons
üîÆ
Premotor Cortex ‚Äî Divisions & Function
Connections
  • Indirect: reciprocal projections to primary motor cortex
  • Direct: axons project into corticobulbar & corticospinal tracts
Lateral Premotor Cortex ‚Äî Closed-Loop
  • Important for externally-cued ("closed-loop") motor tasks
  • Example: monkey trained to reach in different directions depending on visual cue
  • Neurons fire at appearance of cue & maintain elevated firing between cue and signal to move
  • ‚Üí neurons encode intention to move
  • Lesion effect: impairs "closed-loop" tasks even though animal can see cue and can perform the motor response separately
  • Patients with frontal lobe damage: difficulty initiating movements in response to visual cues
Medial Premotor Cortex (SMA) ‚Äî Open-Loop
  • Involved in internally-cued / spontaneous movement selection
  • "Open-loop" conditions
  • Lesion effect: reduces spontaneous movements BUT preserves ability to initiate movement in response to visual cues
Lateral PMC = external cues ¬∑ Medial PMC (SMA) = internal cues / spontaneous

Mirror Motor Neurons ‚Äî Ventrolateral Premotor Cortex
Definition
A mirror motor neuron fires both when an animal acts AND when the animal observes the same action performed by another individual.
Condition A ‚Äî Fires
  • Trainer places food morsel with hand
  • Monkey watches ‚Üí neuron fires
  • Monkey reaches & retrieves ‚Üí neuron fires
  • Monkey reaches behind barrier (can't see own hand) ‚Üí still fires
Condition B ‚Äî Does NOT Fire
  • Food placed with pliers (not hand)
  • Neuron does NOT respond
  • ‚Üí action-specific, not just goal-specific
Implications
  • PMC plays role in encoding observed actions of others
  • Fires even without visual feedback of own movement (behind barrier)
  • Possible basis for imitation learning & social cognition
Recording Method (raster plots + peristimulus histograms)
  • Each tick mark = one action potential; each row = one trial
  • Histograms aligned to observed vs. executed movement onset

Vestibular Nuclei
Medial Vestibulospinal Tract
  • Origin: vestibular complex nuclei
  • Projection: bilateral
  • Mediates feedback ‚Äî responds to disturbances of body posture & stability
  • Input signal: semicircular canals
  • Example: extending arms + dorsiflexion of neck when you trip
Lateral Vestibulospinal Tract
  • Origin: lateral vestibular nucleus
  • Projection: ipsilateral
  • Involved in proximal muscle responses
  • Maintains stable balance & upright posture
  • Input signal: otolith organs (linear acceleration/gravity)
Medial VS = feedback from semicircular canals (rotation). Lateral VS = feedback from otoliths (gravity/tilt).
üï∏Ô∏è
Reticular Formation
Reticulospinal Tract
  • Projects to medial ventral horn
  • Modulates reflexes for stereotyped movement
  • Receives input from: cortex, hypothalamus, brainstem
  • Pontine AND medullary reticular formation
Feedforward Postural Control
  • Cortical UMNs initiate both the primary movement AND a compensatory movement simultaneously
  • Compensatory movement counters predicted destabilization
  • Reticular formation neurons initiate the feedforward adjustments
  • The compensatory movement PRECEDES the primary movement
  • Example: gastrocnemius (leg) contracts before biceps pull on a handle
  • EMG: Gastrocnemius tone rises before Biceps activation
Feedforward = preprogrammed, anticipatory. Feedback = reactive, triggered by sensory detection of instability.

Superior Colliculus & Red Nucleus
Superior Colliculus ‚Üí Colliculospinal Tract
  • Direct pathway neurons to the spinal cord
  • Indirect pathway through reticular formation ‚Üí reticulospinal tract
  • Controls axial muscles in the neck
  • Function: head orientation
  • Projects to cervical spinal cord
Red Nucleus ‚Üí Rubrospinal Tract
  • Projections limited to cervical level of spinal cord only
  • Terminates in lateral regions of ventral horn & intermediate zone
  • Controls arm / hand movements
  • Active prior to movement onset
  • Located in midbrain tegmentum

Feedforward & Feedback Postural Control
Feedforward
  • "Preprogrammed" ‚Äî anticipates instability
  • Typically precedes onset of limb movement
  • Cortical UMN initiates primary movement + compensatory signal simultaneously
  • Reticular formation executes adjustment
Feedback
  • Initiated by sensory inputs that detect postural instability
  • Reactive ‚Äî occurs after disturbance detected
  • Vestibulospinal: main pathway for feedback responses
