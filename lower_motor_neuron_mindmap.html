Organisation of Motor Control Systems
Why do we have brains? ‚ñ∏
Answer: To move and act upon our environment
Movement types: agility, balance, co-ordination, speed, jumping, climbing, walking, skating, hopping, swimming, skipping, throwing, dribbling, kicking, hitting, catching
Four Subsystems that Control Movement ‚ñ∏
Motor Cortex (Upper Motor Neurons / Descending Systems) ‚Äì planning, initiating, directing voluntary movements
Basal Ganglia ‚Äì initiation of intended movement; suppression of unwanted movement ‚Üí Parkinson's & Huntington's Disease
Brainstem Centers ‚Äì rhythmic, stereotyped movements and postural control
Cerebellum ‚Äì coordination of ongoing movement; predicting & correcting motor error; feed-forward controller
Spinal Cord & Brainstem Circuits ‚Üí Local circuit neurons (sensorimotor integration & CPG) ‚Üí Motor neuron pools (Lower motor neurons)
Lower motor neurons = "Final Common Path" for both reflexive and voluntary movement

Distribution of Lower Motor Neurons
Spinal Cord Organisation ‚ñ∏
Located in the ventral horn of spinal cord
Each lower motor neuron innervates muscle fibers within a single muscle
All motor neurons innervating one muscle = motor neuron pool for that muscle
Motor neuron pools are spatially distinct (shown in cat & human)
Human Spatial Organisation ‚ñ∏
Cervical enlargement ‚Üí motor neuron pools innervating the arm
Lumbar enlargement ‚Üí motor neuron pools innervating the leg
Somatotopic Organisation in Ventral Horn ‚ñ∏
Medial motor neuron pools ‚Üí postural trunk muscles (innervated by axial neurons)
Lateral motor neuron pools ‚Üí distal limb muscles & limb motor control
Proximal muscles controlled by intermediate pools
Local Circuit Neurons ‚Äì Somatotopic Connections ‚ñ∏
Long-distance local circuit neurons (red axons) ‚Äì medial ventral horn; control axial muscles; long distance & bilateral; control body position & locomotion; commissural axons cross midline
Short-distance local circuit neurons (orange axons) ‚Äì lateral ventral horn; control distal limbs; short distance & unilateral; mediate fine control of distal limb muscles

Types of Lower Motor Neurons
Œ± (Alpha) Motor Neurons ‚ñ∏
Large diameter axons
Innervate extrafusal muscle fibers (striated muscles)
Generate force for posture and movement
Each neuron branches to terminate on 10‚Äì1000 individual muscle fibers
Œ± MN + muscle fibers it innervates = motor unit
Muscle fibers within a motor unit are spatially distributed in the target muscle
Multiple Œ± MNs innervating same muscle are clustered in same motor nucleus in ventral spinal cord
Œ≥ (Gamma) Motor Neurons ‚ñ∏
Small diameter axons
Innervate intrafusal muscle fibers (muscle spindles)
Spindles = sensory receptors embedded in capsules (fusal/intrafusal)
Send sensory info about muscle length and speed of change in length

Motor Units & Force Regulation
Motor Neuron Size & Force ‚ñ∏
Small Œ± MN ‚Üí few muscle fibers ‚Üí small motor unit ‚Üí small force
Large Œ± MN ‚Üí more muscle fibers ‚Üí larger motor unit ‚Üí greater force
Innervation Ratio (examples) ‚ñ∏
Biceps brachii: 774 Œ± axons, 580,000 fibers, ratio 750
Gastrocnemius (medial): 579 Œ± axons, 1,042,000 fibers, ratio 1,800
Lumbricales (1): 96 Œ± axons, 10,269 fibers, ratio 107
Rectus lateralis (eye): 4,150 Œ± axons, 22,000 fibers, ratio 5 (ultra fine control)
Platysma: 1,096 Œ± axons, 27,100 fibers, ratio 25
Low ratio = fine motor control; High ratio = powerful but coarse
Three Types of Motor Units ‚ñ∏
Slow (S) ‚Äì red, rich in myoglobin & mitochondria; fatigue-resistant; important for sustained activation (upright posture)
Fast fatigue-resistant (FR) ‚Äì intermediate; greater force than S, less than FF; moderate endurance
Fast-fatigable (FF) ‚Äì pale, low mitochondria; easily fatigued; important for forceful rapid movements (running, jumping)
Sprinter ‚Üí more FF units | Marathon runner ‚Üí more S units
Size Principle (Henneman) ‚ñ∏
Small, slow motor units have lower activation thresholds than larger units
Small units are tonically active during sustained effort (e.g. standing)
Large, fast motor units only recruited during high force, rapid movements
Progressive recruitment order: S ‚Üí FR ‚Üí FF
More stimulation ‚Üí larger higher-threshold units recruited ‚Üí more force
Size principle = simple solution to grading muscle force
Recruitment in Cat Medial Gastrocnemius ‚ñ∏
Standing ‚Äì ~5% max force; provided by S units only
Walking ‚Äì ~25% max force; additional recruitment of FR units
Running/Galloping/Jumping ‚Äì full force; additional recruitment of FF units
Stimulation Rate & Muscle Tension ‚ñ∏
Twitch ‚Äì single contraction from one AP in muscle fiber (5 Hz)
Temporal summation ‚Äì overlapping twitches increase force (20 Hz)
Unfused tetanus ‚Äì high frequency, individual twitches still apparent (80 Hz)
Fused tetanus ‚Äì highest rate; twitches indistinguishable; sustained contraction without relaxation (100 Hz)
Under normal conditions: unfused tetanus achieves smooth movement because asynchronous firing of different LMNs averages out tension changes
Both number of active motor units AND their rate of firing increase with voluntary force
Motor Unit Plasticity ‚ñ∏
Ability to acquire new motor skills depends on upper motor neurons and motor units
Chronic electrical stimulation alters motor units at both muscle fiber level and motor neuron phenotype
56 days of chronic stimulation ‚Üí changes in fibre type and firing properties

Muscle Spindles
Two Fibre Types in Skeletal Muscle ‚ñ∏
Extrafusal fibres ‚Äì large diameter; produce movement via large Œ± motor system; bulk of skeletal muscle
Intrafusal fibres ‚Äì small diameter; much shorter than extrafusal fibres; innervated by Œ≥ MNs; regulate muscle length at all times; stretch on extension, shorten on flexion
8‚Äì10 intrafusal fibres grouped in connective tissue capsule = muscle spindle
Spindle is mechanically in parallel to extrafusal fibres
Intrafusal Fibre Sub-types ‚ñ∏
Nuclear bag fibres ‚Äì 2‚Äì3 per spindle; dynamic (db1) and static (sb2) varieties
Nuclear chain fibres ‚Äì 4‚Äì6 per spindle; static signalling
Large diameter sensory axons relay rapid APs in response to stretch
Stretch Receptor Types ‚ñ∏
Group Ia (primary endings) ‚Äì spiral around bag & chain fibres; respond phasically to small stretches (dynamic ‚Äì rate of change)
Group II (secondary endings) ‚Äì mainly on chain fibres; fire tonically, proportional to stretch (static ‚Äì absolute length)
Ia & II Afferent Connections ‚ñ∏
Ia afferents form mono- AND polysynaptic excitatory connections on Œ± MNs
II afferents form polysynaptic excitatory connections on Œ± MNs
The stretch reflex is mediated through this system
Œ≥ Motor Innervation of Spindle ‚ñ∏
Two types of Œ≥ axon: dynamic and static
Dynamic Œ≥ axon ‚Üí terminates on dynamic nuclear bag fibres
Static Œ≥ axon ‚Üí terminates on static bag AND chain fibres
Dynamic bag intrafusal fibre innervated by 1‚Äì2 dynamic Œ≥ axons

Stretch Reflex
Basic Mechanism ‚ñ∏
Stretching muscle spindle ‚Üí ‚Üë activity in Group Ia sensory afferents ‚Üí ‚Üë activity of Œ± MNs that innervate the same muscle
Unique monosynaptic reflex ‚Äì only one synapse between sensory afferent and motor neuron
Ia afferents also excite motor neurons innervating synergistic muscles
Ia afferents indirectly inhibit motor neurons of antagonist muscles via reciprocal Ia inhibitory interneurons (grey neurons)
Reciprocal innervation = contraction of stretched muscle + relaxation of antagonist
Negative Feedback Loop ‚ñ∏
Disturbance (e.g. weight added to held glass) ‚Üí ‚Üë muscle load ‚Üí length change ‚Üí spindle receptor activated ‚Üí ‚Üë spindle afferent discharge ‚Üí ‚Üë Œ± MN activity ‚Üí muscle resists disturbance
Descending facilitation and inhibition modulate the loop
Used to maintain muscle length at a setpoint
Stretch Reflex Gain ‚ñ∏
Gain = amount of force produced in response to a given stretch
High gain ‚Üí small intrafusal stretch ‚Üí large increase in extrafusal tension
Muscle tone = steady tension level; mediated by Group II sensory afferents
Examples of gain modulation: standing on moving bus (gain ‚Üë); doing stretching exercises (gain ‚Üì to allow lengthening)
Œ≥ motor neurons regulate gain by adjusting tension in intrafusal fibres
Gain also depends on excitability of Œ± MNs
Œ± & Œ≥ MNs often co-activated to regulate muscle spindles
Both descending UMN projections AND local spinal circuits can modulate gain by exciting/inhibiting Œ± & Œ≥ MNs
Inhibitory interneurons can regulate Ia afferents via axo-axonal synapses
Œ±‚ÄìŒ≥ Co-activation ‚ñ∏
When only Œ± MN activated ‚Üí muscle shortens ‚Üí intrafusal fibres go slack ‚Üí Ia firing drops (spindle silenced)
When Œ± and Œ≥ MNs activated simultaneously ‚Üí Œ≥ activation keeps intrafusal taut ‚Üí Ia response "filled in" ‚Üí spindle can operate at any muscle length
Movement = combined result of sensory neurons + Œ≥ MNs + Œ± MNs

Golgi Tendon Organs (GTOs)
Structure & Location ‚ñ∏
Located at muscle‚Äìtendon junction
Collagen fibrils inside capsule innervated by a single group of Ib sensory axons
Muscle contraction ‚Üí force ‚Üë tension in collagen fibrils ‚Üí compresses sensory nerve endings
Sensitivity Profile ‚ñ∏
Most sensitive to tension increase from active contraction
Insensitive to passive stretch (contrast with muscle spindles which are tonic during passive stretch)
During passive stretch: spindles signal rapidly (tonic); GTOs signal slowly (phasic)
During active contraction: spindles signal phasically (less); GTOs signal tonically (more)
GTO Reflex Pathways ‚ñ∏
Ib afferents ‚Üí inhibitory interneurons ‚Üí Œ± MNs of SAME muscle ‚Üí negative feedback regulation of muscle tension
Ib afferents ‚Üí excitatory interneurons ‚Üí Œ± MNs of ANTAGONIST muscles ‚Üí ‚Üë activity in opposing muscle
Arrangement protects muscle when exceptionally large force is generated
At lower force levels: GTOs maintain steady tension, counteracting fatigue that would diminish force
Spindle vs GTO Summary ‚ñ∏
Muscle spindles ‚Üí monitor & maintain muscle LENGTH
Golgi tendon organs ‚Üí monitor & maintain muscle FORCE

Locomotion & Central Pattern Generators
Nature of Locomotion ‚ñ∏
Rhythmic movements (walking, running, swimming) controlled by central pattern generators (CPGs) in spinal cord
CPGs can control timing & coordination of complex movement patterns
CPGs can also adjust complex movements in response to altered circumstances
Single Limb Locomotion Cycle ‚ñ∏
Stance phase ‚Äì extending muscle; limb in contact with ground
Swing phase ‚Äì muscle flexed; limb off ground
Speed increases by completing cycle more rapidly
Faster speed ‚Üí reduced stance phase; swing phase remains largely constant
Inter-limb Synchrony in Quadrupeds ‚ñ∏
Walk ‚Äì slow; left-to-right and back-to-front sequential pattern
Trot ‚Äì opposing limbs synchronise (LH‚ÄìRF and LF‚ÄìRH)
Pace ‚Äì ipsilateral limbs move together
Gallop ‚Äì forelimbs synchronised; hindlimbs synchronised; highest speed
Evidence for Spinal CPGs ‚ñ∏
Coordinated locomotion does not require descending projections from higher centres
After spinal cord transection, a supported cat can still walk on a treadmill
Local spinal cord circuits act as CPGs to control flexion & extension during locomotion
