Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

Epidemiology (USA)

  • #1 cause of vision loss in people over 55
  • Affects ~6 million Americans
  • Some forms are also juvenile onset

Pathology

Damages photoreceptors in the macula & fovea ‚Üí loss of high-resolution central vision.

Two Types

üíß Wet AMD (~10%)

Caused by leaky blood vessels behind the retina disturbing the retina‚ÄìRPE connection. More acute & treatable.

üèú Dry AMD (~90%)

Slowly progressing. Drusen deposits accumulate. No good treatments currently available.

Visual Effect

Central scotoma ‚Äî dark/blurry spot blocks the centre of gaze; peripheral vision preserved.

Blindsight

Cause

Damage to primary visual cortex (V1) ‚Üí loss of conscious vision in corresponding visual field region.

The Paradox

Despite cortical blindness, certain "subconscious" forms of vision persist:

Awareness of visual objects Awareness of visual motion

Neural Pathways Involved

Superior Colliculus route

Retinal ganglion cells ‚Üí superior colliculus ‚Üí unconscious orienting / detection

LGN ‚Üí Secondary Areas

LGN signals bypass V1 and reach secondary visual cortices directly

Key insight: Consciousness of vision ‚â† all visual processing. Subcortical and extrastriate pathways maintain functional, unconscious vision even when V1 is destroyed.

Hemispatial Neglect

Cause

Usually arises from strokes or brain injury affecting the right cerebral hemisphere, parietal lobe unilaterally.

Effect

  • The left visual field is neglected (contralateral to injury)
  • The eyes physically see the whole visual scene
  • Patients are unable to perceive half the visual field
  • They are totally unaware of what they are missing ‚Äî no subjective sense of absence

Key Distinction from Blindness

Not a sensory loss ‚Äî the visual information reaches the brain but attention/awareness mechanisms fail to process the neglected hemifield.

MT area damage üéû

Cerebral Akinetopsia

‚Äî Motion Blindness ‚Äî

Cause

Damage to the Middle Temporal area (MT) ‚Äî the visual motion-sensitive cortical region.

Symptom

Moving images appear as a series of slowly refreshing static snapshots ‚Äî motion is not perceived continuously.

Clinical Implications

  • Patients cannot perceive fluid motion ‚Äî crossing streets is dangerous
  • Liquid appears frozen; faces & lip movements freeze between frames
MT / V5: A critical hub in the dorsal "where/how" visual stream, receiving inputs via V1 and also direct LGN magnocellular inputs.

Prosopagnosia

‚Äî Face Blindness ‚Äî

Cause

Arises after damage to, or improper development of, face-selective visual regions in the inferotemporal cortex.

Location

Inferotemporal cortex Ventral stream (high areas) "What" pathway

Symptom

Cannot recognise individual faces ‚Äî even familiar ones, including own reflection. Object recognition & other visual functions may be intact.

Split Brain

‚Äî Interhemispheric Communication ‚Äî

Key Researchers

Roger Sperry (1913‚Äì1994) Michael Gazzaniga (1939‚Äì)

Method

Studied animals & patients in which the corpus callosum was severed (callosotomy ‚Äî done to treat severe epilepsy).

Key Findings

  • Severing the callosum stopped "interocular transfer" ‚Äî learning something with one eye did not transfer to the other eye
  • Left hemisphere required for many forms of language processing
  • Callosum needed to transfer visual information presented to one hemisphere to motor control on the contralateral side of the body
  • Without the callosum: left hand literally may not know what right hand is doing (visual guidance)

Part C ‚Äî Vision Restoration Strategies

Overview of Restoration Approaches

Many different strategies are currently being explored to restore sight to blind or vision-impaired people. They operate at different points in the visual pathway.

Cell therapy Gene therapy Retinal prosthetic implants Optogenetic therapy
Biological replacement üß´

Cell Therapy

Core Principle

Use stem cell approaches to generate new cells that replace diseased or damaged retinal cells.

Two Approaches

üî¨ Individual cell implantation

Grow individual cell types in a dish, then attempt to implant them directly into the patient's retina.

üß© Retinal organoids

Grow full retinal organoids in a dish (self-organising 3D retina-like structure), then implant to replace layers of the retina.

Key Reference

Eiraku et al., 2011: Demonstrated self-organising retinal organoid formation from ES cell aggregates through stages: induction of Rx‚Å∫ neuroepithelium ‚Üí evagination ‚Üí invagination into spherical vesicle ‚Üí formation of neural retina (NR) + retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). Sequence shown over Days 1 ‚Üí 5 ‚Üí 7 ‚Üí 9 in culture.

Challenges

Integration into host retina Synaptic connectivity Immune rejection
Genetic correction üß¨

Gene Therapy

Core Principle

Correct damaging or null mutations by delivering functional copies of the correct gene into target cells.

Delivery Vehicle

Genes are packaged and delivered using adeno-associated viruses (AAVs) ‚Äî small, non-pathogenic vectors that can transduce retinal cells after intravitreal or subretinal injection.

Clinical Example: Luxturna

üíâ Luxturna (voretigene neparvovec)

Treats a rare mutation of RPE65 gene (involved in the visual cycle). Delivered via AAV2 vector. Recently FDA-approved in the USA. Initial price: $850,000 per treatment. Just approved in Canada ‚Äî price currently being negotiated.

Process (from slide figure)

  • Intravitreal injection of AAV vector (e.g. AAV.7m8)
  • Vector transduces rod & cone photoreceptors, RPE cells, bipolar cells, ganglion cells
  • Corrected protein (e.g. retinoschisin) produced & secreted by rod cells

Reference

Scholl et al., 2016

Retinal Prosthetic Implants

‚Äî The "Bionic Eye" ‚Äî

Core Principle

Restore sight via implantation of an electric chip that electrically stimulates retinal ganglion cells.

System Components

  • Head-worn camera system (goggle-based) records the visual scene
  • Signal processing unit convolves the visual signal into a pattern of electrical stimulation
  • Implanted chip on the retina delivers electrical pulses to RGCs

Regulatory Status

FDA approved (USA) Approved in Canada Multiple countries approved

Notable Product

The Argus II (Second Sight) ‚Äî the first FDA-approved retinal prosthesis; featured in the New York Times as "F.D.A. Approves Bionic Eye."

Limitations

Low resolution (~60 electrodes) Requires surviving RGCs Bulky hardware
Molecular photosensitisation ‚ö°

Optogenetic Therapy

Core Principle

Make any remaining cells in the blind retina light-sensitive by delivering light-gated ion channels.

How It Works

  • Deliver optogenetic channel (e.g. Channelrhodopsin, ChrimsonR) to normally light-insensitive neurons via AAV vector
  • Uses same goggle-based system as retinal implants ‚Äî goggles amplify & transform ambient light to activate the channels
  • Unlike implants: no metal electrodes ‚Äî purely biological light-gating

Advantages Over Implants

No surgery for chip placement Higher spatial resolution (cell-level) Mutation-independent Circuit-specific

Target Cell Options

Ganglion cells

Bypasses all upstream retinal processing; simpler but loses ON/OFF coding.

Bipolar cells

Retains some downstream retinal processing ‚Äî ON vs OFF pathways and even direction selectivity can be restored.

Status

Currently in clinical trials

Research Evidence

Berry et al., 2019: In rd1 mouse model of RP, delivering MW-opsin (long-wavelength opsin) to bipolar cells restored vision to near-wildtype levels in pattern discrimination (horizontal vs. vertical bars) and static bar acuity tasks. Rhodopsin delivery to the same cells was not significantly effective (N.S. in bar discrimination), but MW-opsin showed significant improvement (p<0.05). Tests run in a two-chamber avoidance box (36√ó15 cm), location recorded over 15-min trials.

Human Clinical Proof-of-Concept

Sahel et al., Nature Medicine 2021: In a blind RP patient, intraocular injection of AAV encoding ChrimsonR combined with engineered goggles achieved partial recovery of visual function. The patient could perceive, locate, count, and touch objects using the treated eye with goggles. EEG showed object-related cortical activity in visual cortex during perception. Patient could not detect objects before injection or after injection without goggles ‚Äî first reported case of partial recovery in a neurodegenerative disease via optogenetic therapy.
