03 â€” Anatomical Regions
Three Main Parts of the Cerebellum (defined by source of input)
Cerebrocerebellum
  • Receives indirect input from many areas of cerebral cortex
  • Particularly well developed in humans and other primates
  • Regulates highly skilled movement
  • Planning & execution of complex spatial & temporal sequences
  • Example: Speech
Spinocerebellum
  • Receives direct input from spinal cord
  • Lateral part â†’ regulates movement of distal muscles
  • Central part ('Vermis') â†’ regulates movement of proximal muscles & some eye movements
Vestibulocerebellum
  • Oldest part of the cerebellum
  • Located in caudal-inferior lobes
  • Components: Flocculus & Nodulus
  • Receives input from vestibular nuclei in brainstem
  • Maintains posture & balance
  • Controls vestibulo-ocular reflex

09 â€” Cellular Level
Neurons and Circuits of the Cerebellum
Cortical Layers (3 Layers)
  • Molecular layer â€” outermost
    • Contains: Stellate cells, Basket cells, Parallel fiber synapses
  • Purkinje cell layer â€” middle
    • Single row of Purkinje cells
  • Granule cell layer â€” innermost
    • Contains: Granule cells, Golgi cells
    • Also: Mossy fiber inputs arrive here
Two Types of Inputs to Cerebellum
  • Mossy Fibers
    • Arise from many areas in cortex & brainstem
    • Synapse onto granule cells (highly abundant)
    • Granule cells give rise to parallel fibers
    • Parallel fibers have excitatory synapses onto dendritic spines of Purkinje cells
  • Climbing Fibers
    • Arise from inferior olive
    • Contact Purkinje cells directly
    • Excitatory synapses onto Purkinje cells
Purkinje Cells â€” The Output Cells
  • Only output cells of the cerebellar cortex
  • Project to the deep cerebellar nuclei
  • Output is GABAergic inhibitory
  • This inhibition modulates the excitation from mossy fibers and climbing fibers that branch to deep cerebellar nuclei
GABAergic Inhibitory Only cortical output

  • Basket cells â€” inhibitory interneurons in molecular layer; synapse onto Purkinje cell soma
  • Stellate cells â€” inhibitory interneurons in molecular layer; synapse onto Purkinje dendrites
  • Golgi cells â€” inhibitory interneurons in granule cell layer; regulate granule cell activity
  • Granule cells â€” excitatory; most numerous neurons in the brain

10 â€” Clinical Implications
Damage to the Cerebellum
General Consequences of Damage
  • Causes persistent errors in movement
  • Errors are on the same side of the body as the cerebellar deficit (ipsilateral)
  • Somatotopic organization means only some muscle groups may be affected
  • Alcohol abuse can cause cerebellar damage â€” particularly in the anterior portion
  • Ataxia: difficulty producing smooth, well-coordinated movement

Other Clinical Signs
  • Dysmetria â€” over or under reaching a target
  • Action / Intention tremors â€” tremors during voluntary movement
  • Speech deficits â€” due to impaired coordination of speech muscles
  • Impaired tandem walking â€” difficulty with heel-to-toe walking
Clinical Context: Inferior Olive Hypertrophy
  • Patient with inferior olive hypertrophy shows impaired motor adaptation
  • Cannot properly adapt to prismatic displacement during throwing task
  • After-effects are absent (no de-adaptation), showing failure of cerebellar learning

Prism Adaptation Experiment
  • Task: Throwing darts at a target while wearing prism glasses
  • Prisms shift visual field â†’ causes initial errors in horizontal displacement

    • No correction during prism exposure
